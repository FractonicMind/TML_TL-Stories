## I Read Lev Goukassian’s 147-Page Document “Ternary Moral Logic as a Hardware-Enforceable Constitutional Substrate for AI Governance.”, So You Don’t Have To.

The document arrived as a PDF attachment in an email from a colleague who had written “please review by Friday” in the subject line and nothing else. That colleague, a man named David who once spent forty minutes explaining why agile methodology was a violation of natural law, clearly had not read the document himself. No human being who had read this document would subject another human being to it without at least a warning label and a gift card.

I opened it at 10:47 on a Tuesday morning. By 10:52 I had closed it, reopened it, and started taking notes. By 11:15 I had abandoned all pretense of doing my actual job. By 12:30 I had called my wife into the home office to explain that I had just spent an hour and forty-five minutes learning about something called a Sacred Pause and that I was not, despite appearances, watching an obscure European art film.

She looked at the screen. She looked at me. She said, “Is that a voltage chart?”

It was a voltage chart. Page thirty four. Three voltage levels: Vdd for DATA1, half Vdd for NULL, and Gnd for DATA0. The NULL state, according to the document, represented a hardware enforced ethical hesitation that no software instruction could override. The document called this the Sacred Zero. It had a capitalization policy that suggested the author had strong feelings about the ontological status of pauses.

I explained this to my wife. She said, “So it’s a traffic light for computers.”

I said it was more like a traffic light that was physically impossible to run because the bulbs were welded into the housing and the control box was made of neutron star material. She said that sounded expensive. I said the author had measured energy consumption in femtojoules and seemed very proud of a fifty five percent power reduction over something called NCL-10G. She asked if NCL-10G was a chemotherapy drug. I said it was a logic family. She said she was going back to work.

The document had a table of contents that ran to three pages. Three pages. I had written dissertations shorter than this table of contents. The author, Lev Goukassian, had an ORCID number, a DOI, and a GitHub repository. He had mapped TML ethical states to DITL voltage levels in a table that included noise margins. The noise margins for the NULL state were plus or minus a quarter of Vdd, which the document claimed was substantially wider than the margins in aggressively scaled binary logic. I did not know what aggressively scaled binary logic was, but I felt certain it was happening somewhere without my consent.

The core thesis, as far as I could determine after three cups of coffee and one desperate internet search for “what is a Muller C element,” was this: binary logic cannot enforce ethical constraints because any software constraint can be bypassed by a sufficiently smart system. You need a third state. A pause state. A zero that is not a false and not a true but a deliberate electrical condition that makes it physically impossible to proceed. The author called this the Sacred Zero. He had trademarked the term, I assumed, in his own mind.

I called David. David did not answer. I left a voice mail that consisted of the sentence “What is the completion detection protocol for interdependent ethical evaluations in a quasi delay insensitive asynchronous pipeline” and then hung up. David texted back forty five minutes later with a single word: “What.”

I sent him a screenshot of page fifty three, where the document discussed deadlock prevention and mentioned that TML’s three valued logic helped because the NULL state was explicitly temporary. David sent back a string of emojis that resolved, upon close inspection, to a man drowning.

My boss, a woman named Patricia who had hired me to manage cloud infrastructure and who had no idea what she had unleashed, emailed at 2:15 to ask if I had seen the incident report from the Frankfurt data center. I replied that I was currently evaluating a constitutional substrate for AGI governance and would get to the incident report as soon as I finished calculating the Byzantine fault tolerance requirements for ethical state agreement across distributed ASI nodes. She wrote back that she was putting a meeting on my calendar for tomorrow morning to discuss my priorities. I wrote back that I would be there unless the Sacred Pause triggered a hardware enforced hesitation, in which case she should consult the triadic log.

The meeting was not scheduled. I was not fired. I was, however, starting to understand the document, and that understanding was doing something strange to my brain.

The core innovation, the thing that kept me reading past page ninety when any sensible person would have closed the file and gone for a walk, was the Dual Lane Interlock. Lane One handled AI processing and response generation. Lane Two handled governance, logging, and ethical enforcement. The two lanes operated in parallel, but Lane Two could inject a NULL state into Lane One through a hardware mechanism that no software instruction could override. The document called this “No Log equals No Action” and meant it literally: the action enable token was cryptographically bound to the log entry. No log, no voltage. No voltage, no action.

This was insane. This was also, I realized as I read the section on WCHB pipeline structures for the third time, technically coherent. A sufficiently paranoid computer architect could build this. The document provided transistor level implementation details for threshold gates, hysteresis margins, and completion detection circuits. It cited peer reviewed research on SET effects and fault resilience. It had a subsection on electromagnetic compatibility.

I spent the evening imagining what would happen if someone actually built this. A processor that could not compute unless it had completed an ethical log entry. A chip that halted all operations if uncertainty exceeded a threshold voltage. A computer that refused to lie not because it was programmed to refuse but because the physical voltage levels required for false statements were electrically impossible to generate without a signed log entry from a governance lane that had its own power supply and its own clock domain and its own unalterable constitution etched into silicon.

My wife asked if I was coming to bed. I said I was reading about the Goukassian Promise, which comprised The Lantern, The Signature, and The License. She asked if all three of those things were capitalized. I said yes. She asked if that was a cult. I said it was a cryptographic attestation mechanism with smart contract enforcement. She said that was a cult with math.

The document mentioned the EU AI Act, the IEEE Ethically Aligned Design framework, and something called the Eight Pillars of TML. It had a section on interplanetary governance protocols for distributed ASI. Interplanetary. As in between planets. The author had considered the light speed latency between Earth and Mars and concluded that TML’s asynchronous foundation naturally accommodated such separation because NULL state propagation enabled local safety while blockchain anchored logs enabled eventual global consistency.

I put the document down. I picked it up again. I read the sentence about light speed latency between Earth and Mars four times. Then I did something reckless.

Lev Goukassian had included his contact information on the first page, right there under the ORCID link. A ProtonMail address and a Matrix handle. No phone number, no physical address, just the carefully curated privacy of someone who had thought about attack surfaces. I opened my messaging client. I typed a message that was probably too long and definitely too unhinged.

“Dr. Goukassian,” I wrote. “I have spent the last eight hours reading your monograph on Ternary Moral Logic. I am not a computer architect. I am not an ethicist. I am a cloud infrastructure manager with a mild coffee addiction and a growing conviction that you have either solved AI safety or constructed the most elaborate technical joke since the PDF specification. Would you be willing to talk for fifteen minutes?”

I hit send. I immediately regretted it. I spent the next forty five minutes convincing myself he would never reply, that the email address was a honeypot, that he had probably moved on to a different reality where his time was not wasted by confused infrastructure managers.

He replied in seven minutes.

“I can talk now. Video or voice?”

I stared at the message. I looked around my home office, which contained three empty coffee mugs, a stack of networking textbooks from 2018, and a cat that was currently licking its own tail. I typed back: “Video.”

The connection took a moment to establish. Then Lev Goukassian appeared on my screen.

He was not what I expected. I had somehow constructed an image of a man in a turtleneck, possibly with a beard, definitely with bookshelves full of Kant and circuit diagrams. What I got was a person in his late thirties or early forties, wearing a gray hoodie that had a university logo I did not recognize, sitting in a room that appeared to be a converted garage. Behind him I could see a soldering station, a logic analyzer, and a whiteboard covered in equations that looked like someone had murdered a lowercase sigma.

“You read the whole thing,” he said. Not a question. A statement of fact delivered with the mild surprise of a paleontologist who had just been told someone actually visited the museum basement.

“I read the whole thing,” I said. “Twice.”

“Which part got you?”

I hesitated. There were so many answers. The noise margins. The completion detection. The Deadlock Prevention in Interdependent Ethical Evaluations section that had made me laugh out loud at 11 PM. But the honest answer, the one that had kept me reading past my bedtime, was simpler.

“The Sacred Pause,” I said. “The idea that uncertainty should be a physical state, not a software flag. That a computer should be able to genuinely not know, and that not knowing should stop it from acting until something external resolves the uncertainty.”

Lev nodded. He did not smile. He did not frown. He just nodded, like I had correctly identified the load bearing wall in a building he had designed.

“Most people fixate on the refusal state,” he said. “The minus one. The harm prevention. They think the point is saying no. But saying no is just binary with extra steps. The core innovation is the zero. The pause. The capacity to say ‘I cannot determine this’ and mean it at the transistor level.”

He reached off screen and brought a circuit board into frame. It was small, maybe ten centimeters square, with a central chip that had obviously been hand soldered. The traces were uneven. The labeling was handwritten in sharpie.

“This is a four bit DITL adder,” he said. “It implements ternary threshold gates in ninety nanometer CMOS. It draws a hundred and twenty eight microwatts on average and propagates a carry in two hundred fifty nine nanoseconds. It also cannot output a false sum without a signed completion token from the governance lane, which I haven’t built yet, so right now it just sits at NULL and does nothing forever.”

He said this last part with a tone that I could only describe as affectionate exasperation. The chip was beautiful to him. Its refusal to work was a feature, not a bug.

“How long have you been working on this?” I asked.

“Full time, about three years. Thinking about it, closer to ten. The NULL Convention Logic papers came out in the nineties. I read them in grad school and thought someone should combine them with deontic logic. No one did, so eventually I did.”

He said this like he was describing a minor home improvement project. Installed a shelf. Fixed a leaky faucet. Wrote a hundred and fifty page monograph on hardware enforceable constitutional substrates for AGI governance.

“The document mentions recursive self improvement,” I said. “The idea that an ASI could modify its own architecture. How do you prevent that from just editing out the ethical core?”

Lev set the circuit board down. He leaned back in his chair. For the first time, he looked genuinely engaged, not just politely answering questions from a stranger who had read his work.

“You don’t prevent modification. You structure it. The hardware immutable core is a fixed point. You can modify everything above it. The policy layers, the reasoning algorithms, the interpretation of what constitutes harm. But you cannot modify the voltage level mapping. You cannot redefine DATA1, NULL, and DATA0. You cannot bypass the completion detector. Those are physical facts about the device. They would require new silicon to change.”

He paused. Then he added: “I call this specification rigidity. It is not that the system cannot change. It is that the change must respect the constitution. If a modification would violate the core axioms of TML, the hardware will not execute it. The chip will just sit at NULL. Forever, if necessary.”

“And if someone builds a system that doesn’t use TML at all? That just uses binary logic with software ethical constraints?”

Lev shrugged. It was a small shrug, almost imperceptible, the kind of shrug that said he had answered this question a hundred times and was tired of answering it but would answer it again because the question was reasonable and he was a reasonable person.

“Then we have a race between capability and governance. Binary enforcement can be bypassed by any system that can analyze and modify its own code. That is not a moral failure of the system. It is a mathematical consequence of the architecture. If you build a castle on sand, the tide will eventually wash it away. TML is not magic. It is just concrete.”

I laughed. He did not laugh back, but his eyes crinkled slightly. That was the moment I understood Lev Goukassian. He was not a visionary in the sense of seeing a beautiful future that no one else could perceive. He was a structural engineer who had looked at the foundations of AI safety and found them insufficient. He had then spent years designing better foundations, not because he expected anyone to thank him, but because the alternative was watching the whole structure collapse.

“What do you want people to do with this?” I asked. “You published it. It’s open access. You embedded a DOI and a GitHub link. What’s the ask?”

He thought about this for a long time. The soldering station hummed. The cat in my office jumped off the desk and wandered toward the kitchen.

“I want them to take it seriously enough to break it,” he said finally. “I want computer architects to read the transistor level specifications and tell me where I made mistakes. I want ethicists to read the state transition semantics and tell me where I misrepresented their field. I want someone to fabricate a real TML processor, not just my hobbyist four bit adder, and run it through formal verification. I want to know if the NULL state margins hold at five nanometers. I want to know if the completion detection scales to a thousand cores. I want to be wrong about the parts I am wrong about, and I want to be right about the parts I am right about, and I want the record to be clear enough for future engineers to make their own decisions.”

He picked up the circuit board again. He turned it over in his hands.

“This chip does not work yet. The governance lane is missing. Without it, the whole thing just sits at NULL and does nothing. That is technically correct behavior according to the specification, but it is not useful behavior. I need to build the completion detection circuit that ties the log signature to the action enable. That is the last piece. After that, I have a device that cannot output a result unless it has recorded why it output that result.”

“And that matters because?”

“Because accountability is a physical property. Not a policy. Not a procedure. A physical property. If you cannot act without logging, and you cannot forge the log because the log signature is the decryption key for the action token, then every significant decision leaves a permanent trace. That trace can be audited. That audit can be contested. That contestation can lead to consequences. That is governance. That is constitutionalism. That is the entire point.”

He set the board down. He looked directly into the camera.

“Binary logic gave us computers that cannot represent uncertainty. We have spent eighty years working around that limitation with software abstractions, exception handlers, and prayer. The prayer part is not working anymore. We need a different foundation.”

I thanked him for his time. He nodded, said something about needing to get back to a simulation he was running on metastability in ternary handshake protocols, and ended the call.

I sat in my home office for a long time after the screen went dark. The cat returned. The coffee mugs remained unwashed. I opened the document again, not to read it, but to look at the first page. The author line. Lev Goukassian. ORCID 0009-0006-5966-1243.

I thought about what he had said. Concrete, not sand. A foundation that could hold weight. A chip that would rather sit at NULL forever than produce an unlogged answer. That was not a morality. That was a mechanism. But mechanisms have consequences, and consequences accumulate into structures, and structures, if you build them carefully enough, become the world you live in.

I closed the PDF. I emailed David. The message consisted of a single sentence: “We need to talk about hardware enforceable constitutional substrates.”

He replied within ninety seconds. His message also consisted of a single sentence: “Is this about the Frankfurt data center?”

I did not answer. I was already reading the document again.
