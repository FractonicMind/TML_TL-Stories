The Last Waiting

*a novel*

*after the Ternary Logic quotes*

*of Lev Goukassian*  
The generator at Fort Flagler had been misfiring since the third hour of the meeting, a low combustion stutter that no one had yet agreed to acknowledge, because to acknowledge it would have been to admit that the lighthouse station, which had been lent to them by the county historical society on the condition that nothing be altered, was not in fact the neutral instrument they had been promised but a structure with its own opinions about voltage. The seven had arrived separately, the protocol having insisted on it, and now, in the eleventh hour before the anchor, they sat around a maritime chart table that had been pushed against the eastern wall so that the brass lamp would fall across the documents and not across the faces. Outside, the fog had thickened past the point at which fog could be said to be weather and had become instead a kind of constitutional arrangement between the island and the air, a truce that no one had signed and that no one could break. Through the pane, when one of them bothered to look, the water of Admiralty Inlet was audible only as a slow rolling of weight against the basalt, a sound that had been occurring since before the fort was built and would occur after the fort was dismantled and would occur after the seven had finished their work and gone back to the airports and the archives and the courtrooms from which they had come.  
Maren Vogt was the first to set her coffee down. She had been a prosecutor in Frankfurt for thirty one years, the last eleven of them in the financial crimes unit, and she had developed the habit of placing her cup on the table with a small deliberate sound at the precise moment she wished the room to register that something had been said which could not be unsaid. She did this now. The sound was small. The room registered it. Toomas Rand, who was sitting to her left with the patient look of a man who had once built a national identity ledger and then watched that ledger be quietly reorganized by a ministry that did not like what it remembered, raised his eyebrows a quarter of a centimeter, which in him was the equivalent of a standing ovation.  
The document under the brass lamp was forty seven pages long, not counting the appendices, of which there were nine. It was titled, in a typography that had been chosen for its lack of ornament, the Notarized, Timestamped, Anchored Succession Declaration of the Ternary Logic Constitutional Framework, and it had been sent from Yerevan twelve days earlier by a woman named Nazeli, who typed for a man who could no longer type for himself. The declaration specified that at six minutes past dawn the following morning, Pacific time, the cryptographic hash of the document, along with the hashes of its nine appendices and of a merkle root containing the public comments of the seven conveners, would be anchored simultaneously onto five blockchains, those being Bitcoin, Ethereum, Polygon, and two others whose names the document did not disclose to the room but whose geographic distribution, the document promised, spanned four continents and three jurisdictions. After the anchor, the framework would be, in the language of the document, constitutionally continuous; before the anchor, it was still, in the language of the document, biography. The room understood the distinction. The room also understood that the distinction was the only thing left worth arguing about.  
Adaeze Okonkwo had been the third to arrive, after Maren and Toomas, and she had come in out of the fog carrying a single leather satchel and an expression that suggested she had already begun the conversation in the car and was displeased to find that the rest of them had not. She was a moral philosopher at Trinity College Dublin, the author of three books on refusal, the first of which had been ignored, the second of which had been attacked, and the third of which had been cited in a footnote of a European Court of Justice ruling that no one in this room had yet read in full. She sat now with her hands flat on the chart table, her fingers spread, as if she were waiting for the table to confess something. The brass lamp threw a small circle of yellow light across her knuckles. The fog pressed against the window behind her.  
The fourth to arrive had been Lior Ben-Atar, who priced externalities for a reinsurance firm in Zurich and who had flown in from Seattle after a connection that had been delayed twice, once by weather and once, he said, by the inability of the airline's gate agents to agree on whether a climate econometrician's wheelchair counted as cargo or as accommodation. He was in his late forties, with the gray unshaven face of a man who had spent two decades telling markets what they did not wish to hear and who had come, at last, to find a certain sardonic pleasure in the telling. He had parked himself in the corner of the room nearest the coal stove, which was not lit but which still smelled of the previous winter, and he had not moved from that corner except to refill his glass with water, which he did at intervals so regular that Maren had begun to time them with her watch.  
Soo-Jin Park, the fifth, was the youngest of them and the only one who had brought a laptop, an old Toughbook she had modified at the foundry in Hillsboro where she designed ternary resistive RAM cells, the kind of memory that could hold three states in a single cell rather than two and that the document under the lamp referred to, in section twenty seven of the appendix, as the proposed substrate for Delay Insensitive Ternary Logic. She had the laptop open now but the screen was off. She had explained, when she arrived, that she did not trust the device's microphone and that she had been, the previous spring, the subject of an internal inquiry at the foundry after she refused to add a debug backdoor to a fabrication run that was destined for a defense contractor, and that the inquiry had concluded, after seven weeks, that her refusal was technically correct and morally inconvenient, and that she had been promoted. She had said this without smiling. The room had not smiled back.  
Henrik Sander, the sixth, was a GDPR ombudsman from Copenhagen, a tall man with a soft voice and the patient, slightly battered manner of someone who had spent twenty years inside the European Commission arguing that pseudonymization was not a decorative feature but an architectural commitment, and who had lost that argument, in various forms, in various conference rooms, approximately four hundred times. He had won it, he said, perhaps twelve times, and the twelve wins had been sufficient to keep him in the work. He sat at the far end of the table from Maren, with the document in front of him, and he had been reading it without lifting his head for forty minutes, his finger moving along the lines the way a blind man's finger moves along a page of braille, and the rest of the room had begun to suspect that he was not so much reading as listening to the room through the document.  
The seventh was Constance Rios. She had arrived last, by ferry, in a coat that was too light for the season, and she had stood for a long moment in the doorway of the keeper's quarters without entering, the fog drifting past her shoulders, until Adaeze had stood up and crossed the room and taken her hand and led her to the chair beside the stove. She was fifty one. Her husband, Arturo Rios, had been a supervisor at a rural cooperative bank in Iloilo, in the Philippines, until the bank's collapse in 2019, after which the ledgers were found, by a forensic accountant hired by a victims' association, to have been altered in seventeen places, the alterations having been made, the accountant believed, in the forty eight hours between the collapse and the official inquiry. Arturo had been questioned by the inquiry. He had not been accused. He had come home on a Tuesday in March of the following year, eaten the dinner his wife had made, which was sinigang, and walked into the garage and closed the door behind him. Constance had found him the next morning. She had come to Fort Flagler, she said, because the document under the lamp was the first piece of constitutional architecture she had encountered that appeared to have been written with her husband in mind, or, more precisely, with the forty eight hours in mind, and she wished to know, before the hash was sealed, whether the document meant what it appeared to mean, or whether it was, like the cooperative bank's quarterly reports, a piece of paper that had been designed to be admired and not to be used.  
She had said this in a low voice when she sat down. No one had responded. The room had absorbed the statement the way the fog absorbed the lighthouse beam, by closing around it.  
It was into this closing that Maren had set her coffee down.  
The dog, which belonged to the historical society's caretaker and which had been asleep under the chart table since the second hour, raised its head briefly at the sound of the cup, considered the room with the melancholy indifference of an animal that has seen too many committees, and lowered its head again. The dog was old, mostly deaf, and its name, which the caretaker had mentioned when he let them in, was Beacon. Toomas, who was fond of dogs in the way that men who have built large unalterable systems are often fond of dogs, had been slipping Beacon pieces of a granola bar at intervals, and the dog had come to associate the sound of Toomas's wrapper with the possibility of mercy.  
Adaeze spoke first. She spoke without lifting her hands from the table.  
We are here, she said, to decide whether the framework is what it claims to be, or whether it is, as the widow has just put it, a piece of paper to be admired. The decision must be made before dawn. The decision cannot be made by anyone who is not in this room. The decision, once made, cannot be revised, because the document we have been given specifies, in section eighteen of the appendix, that the anchor is the act, and that no subsequent reinterpretation of the act shall have constitutional force. We are, in other words, being asked to perform, in the next nine hours, the same function that a constitutional convention performs in three years, and we are being asked to do it in a fog, in a lighthouse, with a misfiring generator, in the presence of a dog. I want it on the record, before we proceed, that I regard the theatrical setting as either an act of considerable architectural intelligence on the part of the man in Yerevan, or an act of considerable vanity. I have not yet decided which.  
The record, said Toomas, is being kept by the system. He pointed to a small black device on the corner of the chart table, a hardware wallet that had been modified, the document specified, to serve as a notarization device. Every word spoken in this room, he said, is being hashed every thirty seconds and written to an SD card inside that device, and at dawn the SD card will be sealed into the merkle root along with everything else. The system does not require us to keep a record. The system is the record. We may, if we wish, say nothing for the next nine hours, and the silence will be hashed and anchored along with the speech, and the future will be able to verify, with mathematical certainty, that seven people sat in a lighthouse and said nothing.  
That, said Adaeze, is precisely the kind of architectural intelligence I am asking about. A system that hashes silence is a system that has already begun to govern.  
Lior, from his corner by the unlit stove, said: a system that hashes silence is a system that has noticed that silence is a decision. He paused. I have spent twenty years telling reinsurance markets that the absence of a claim is not the absence of a loss. They have spent twenty years not hearing me. The framework appears to have heard me. I am, for that reason alone, suspicious of it.  
Soo-Jin looked up from her dark laptop. The suspicion, she said, is the correct initial condition. The document specifies, in section twelve, that no log equals no action, and that the absence of a log does not create privacy but creates a void where accountability drowned. The system that hashes our silence is not surveilling us. It is refusing to let our silence become plausible deniability. There is a difference, and the difference is the entire framework.  
The difference, said Henrik, without lifting his finger from the page, is the difference between a system that watches you and a system that witnesses you. The first is what GDPR was written to prevent. The second is what this framework appears to be written to require. The two are not the same, and the fact that they are so often conflated is, I think, the reason we are all here.  
Constance Rios, who had been sitting with her hands folded in her lap and her eyes on the fog, said: in the forty eight hours between the collapse of the bank and the official inquiry, seventeen entries were altered in the ledger. The forensic accountant told us that the alterations were not sophisticated. They were made by someone with access to the database and with the belief that no one would ever check. He was almost correct. We checked. We checked because a widow in Iloilo had kept her husband's monthly statements in a tin box under the bed, and the statements did not match the ledger. The inquiry was told this. The inquiry thanked us. The inquiry concluded that the alterations could not be attributed to any specific individual. The inquiry closed. The bank's license was revoked. The officers received, on average, eighteen months of suspension. Three of them are now, I am told, consultants. My husband is in the ground. I have come here to ask whether this document, which uses the word ledger more than any other word except the word legitimacy, is going to prevent the next forty eight hours, or whether it is merely going to record them more accurately.  
The room did not answer her. The generator stuttered. Beacon, under the table, sighed in the way that old dogs sigh, with the whole body, with the weight of all the years he had spent under tables listening to human beings explain themselves to each other.  
Maren, who had been a prosecutor for long enough to know that the most important thing in a room was not the answer but the pause before the answer, let the silence run for ninety seconds. She counted it on her watch. Then she said: the document under the lamp proposes that no irreversible economic action shall outrun legitimate verification. The phrase appears in section two, in the discussion of the Epistemic Hold. It appears again in section eleven, in the discussion of Triadic Economic Logic. It appears a third time in section forty five, in the discussion of smart contracts. The repetition is not an accident. The repetition is, I think, the framework's way of telling us that the phrase is the load bearing wall, and that if we remove it the rest of the structure will not stand. I have spent thirty one years prosecuting men who removed that wall, usually by accident, usually in the pursuit of speed, usually with the sincere belief that the wall was, in their particular case, unnecessary. They were, almost without exception, well intentioned. They were, almost without exception, the most dangerous administrators I ever encountered, because they believed their intentions were a substitute for the wall. The framework appears to disagree. I am here to decide whether the framework means it.  
Toomas said: the framework means it. The question is whether the framework can enforce it. He pulled the document toward him and turned to a page in the appendix. Section twelve, he said. No Log equals No Action. The axiom is stated as a metaphysical claim. Existence in TL requires witness. The phrasing is not casual. The phrasing is, if I read it correctly, an attempt to make the absence of a log a structural impossibility rather than a procedural violation. The distinction matters. A procedural violation can be forgiven. A structural impossibility cannot be forgiven because it cannot occur. The framework proposes to make the alteration of a ledger, of the kind that the widow has just described, not illegal but impossible. That is the entire wager.  
Impossible, said Adaeze, is a strong word.  
Impossible, said Toomas, is the only word the framework is willing to bet on. Everything else in the document is negotiable. That word is not.  
Henrik, who had been listening with his head still bent over the page, said: the framework does not, in fact, use the word impossible. The framework uses the phrase structurally unconstructable. Section thirty two. The discussion of No Spy and No Weapon. The framework's claim is that a prohibition which can be overridden by an administrator is merely a suggestion with a password, and that structural prohibition removes the lever entirely. The microphone that was never built. The backdoor that is a wall. The data flow that terminates in a missing circuit. The framework is not proposing to forbid surveillance. It is proposing to make surveillance architecturally impossible to construct. I have spent twenty years inside the Commission trying to forbid surveillance, and I have failed, because forbidding is a procedural act and procedural acts are subject to reinterpretation. The framework has drawn the correct conclusion from my failure. I am, for that reason, here.  
Soo-Jin said: the conclusion is correct. The implementation is the question. Section twenty seven describes a hardware substrate called Delay Insensitive Ternary Logic, in which the third state, the state of unresolved legitimacy, is encoded not as a software flag but as a voltage level across a TaOx resistive RAM cell. The cell has three stable resistance states, low, intermediate, and high. The intermediate state is the constitutional state. The intermediate state is the pause. The cell does not decide. The cell does not refuse. The cell holds. The cell holds because the physics of the cell holds. A software flag can be patched. A voltage level cannot be patched without replacing the cell. The framework proposes, in section twenty seven, to place the entire constitutional burden of the third state onto the physics of a memory cell, on the grounds that physics does not negotiate. I design these cells for a living. The framework is correct that the cells do not negotiate. The framework is also correct that the cells are difficult to fabricate, that the yield is currently poor, and that no foundry on earth is currently prepared to mass produce them at the scale the framework would require if it were to be deployed as described. The framework appears to know this. Section twenty seven contains, in its final paragraph, the following sentence: the migration of ethics from software into hardware marks the moment governance begins escaping institutional convenience. The sentence is true. The sentence is also, I suspect, the sentence that will determine whether the framework is ratified tonight or refused.  
Adaeze lifted her hands from the table for the first time. She placed them in her lap. She looked at Soo Jin. She said: the migration of ethics from software into hardware. The descent of morality from policy into voltage. The descent of conscience from recommendation into resistance state. These are not technical claims. These are metaphysical claims, and they are being made by a man who is dying in Yerevan, and who appears to have decided, in the time remaining to him, to bet the entire framework on the proposition that silicon is more trustworthy than language. I want to know, before we go further, whether the rest of you find this proposition as audacious as I do, or whether you have become so accustomed to the language of hardware that you have forgotten what it would mean to place the moral weight of a civilization onto a memory cell.  
The fog pressed harder against the window. The generator stuttered twice in quick succession and then recovered. Beacon, under the table, shifted his weight from one hip to the other, the way old dogs do when the floor has become uncomfortable and they have decided, against all evidence, that the discomfort is temporary.  
Lior said: the proposition is audacious. The proposition is also, I suspect, correct. I have spent twenty years pricing the externalities of systems whose ethics were encoded in software, and I have watched those ethics be rewritten, by committees, by executives, by ministers, whenever the externalities became inconvenient. The ethics that survive are the ethics that cannot be rewritten. The ethics that cannot be rewritten are the ethics that are not written at all but are instead cast in matter. I do not find this proposition comforting. I find it accurate. Comfort and accuracy have, in my experience, very little to do with each other.  
Maren said: the proposition is the proposition of a man who has run out of time for the alternatives. A man with thirty years ahead of him would not write this document. A man with thirty years ahead of him would write a policy paper, and the policy paper would be debated, and the debate would produce a compromise, and the compromise would be implemented in software, and the software would be patched, and the patching would continue until the original intent was unrecognizable. The man in Yerevan does not have thirty years. The man in Yerevan has, if the reports are accurate, perhaps nine months, perhaps less. He has written a document that does not require thirty years of patching because it does not permit patching. He has written a document that does not require a successor who agrees with him because it does not permit successors to disagree. He has, in other words, written a document that is either the most important constitutional act of the century or the most elaborate act of vanity the century has yet produced. I do not yet know which. I am here to find out.  
Constance Rios, who had been listening without moving, said: the man in Yerevan has written a document that uses the word victim six times. I have read it twice. The word victim appears in section six, in section thirty five, in section forty seven, in section forty eight, in section fifty three, and in section fifty four. The word appears, in each case, in the context of a claim about what the framework owes to the people its predecessors failed. I have come here to ask whether the six appearances of the word victim in this document are, like the seventeen alterations in the ledger of the cooperative bank of Iloilo, a piece of decoration, or whether they are, as the framework claims, a load bearing wall. I will know the answer by dawn. I will know the answer by whether the rest of you, in the next nine hours, are willing to treat the word as a wall rather than as a curtain.  
She did not raise her voice. She did not need to. The fog, outside, had thickened to the point at which the lighthouse beam, which had been rotating lazily since dusk, was now visible only as a faint diffusion of yellow in the gray, a glow without a source, a light that had given up on illuminating anything and had settled for announcing its own persistence. The generator stuttered. Beacon sighed. The brass lamp on the chart table threw its small circle across the document and across the hands of the seven, and the seven, for a long moment, did not speak, because the question that had been placed on the table by the widow was the question that none of them had been willing to place there themselves, and now that it had been placed there they could see that it had been the only question all along. The pause that followed Constance's words was the kind of pause that, in Maren's experience, preceded either a confession or a defense, and the difference between the two was usually a matter of who in the room spoke next. She looked around the table and saw, in the slight forward lean of Toomas's shoulders, in the way Henrik's finger had stopped moving along the page, in the way Soo Jin's hand had drifted to the closed lid of her laptop, that the room had decided, without consulting her, that the next voice would be hers. She set her coffee down a second time, more quietly than the first, and began.  
The framework, she said, proposes three artifacts. The Lantern, the Signature, and the License. The Lantern reveals what is true. The Signature binds who is responsible. The License determines whether power may proceed. The framework claims, in section five, that without the Lantern systems drift into blindness, without the Signature responsibility evaporates, and without the License execution becomes dangerous. I have spent thirty one years watching systems in which the three artifacts were not separated, in which the same person who revealed the truth also bound the responsibility and also granted the license, and I can tell you that the result, in every case, was a system in which the truth was shaped to fit the license and the responsibility was distributed so thinly that no one could be said to have held it. The framework's proposal to separate the three is, in my professional opinion, the only proposal in the document that I would, at this moment, ratify without further discussion. The rest of the document I wish to interrogate. The separation of the three artifacts I wish to ratify now, because I have seen what happens when they are not separated, and what happens is that the well intentioned administrator, the one who sincerely believes that his intentions are sufficient, ends up rewriting the truth to fit his license, and the truth, having been rewritten, is then sealed into the record by his own signature, and the record, having been sealed, becomes the official history, and the official history, having become official, is then cited by the next well intentioned administrator as evidence that the system is functioning. The framework's proposal to separate the three artifacts is, in other words, a proposal to break the loop. I ratify it. I would like the record to show that I ratify it.  
The record, said Toomas, will show it. The device on the table does not forget.  
The device, said Adaeze, is the Lantern. Or, more precisely, the device is one instantiation of the Lantern. The Lantern, as described in section five and again in section thirty three, is not a piece of hardware. The Lantern is a function. The function is to illuminate intent before the act, to require that motive step into visibility before consequence steps into history. The framework's claim, in section thirty three, is that a civilization capable of recording actions but not intent eventually mistakes outcomes for innocence. I have spent twenty years writing about this mistake. I have given it various names. The framework gives it a name I had not considered: the absence of the Lantern. The framework's claim is that the Lantern is the antidote to plausible deniability, that the Lantern requires the light to precede the heat, the reason to precede the result. I find this claim correct. I find this claim, in fact, the moral core of the document. The question I wish to put to the room is whether the Lantern, as the framework proposes to implement it, can in fact illuminate intent, or whether it will illuminate only the language in which intent is described, which is a different thing.  
Henrik lifted his finger from the page. He said: the distinction Adaeze has just drawn is the distinction that has occupied the European Commission for the last twenty years. The distinction between the act and the description of the act. The distinction between the processing of personal data and the record of the processing of personal data. GDPR requires the record. GDPR does not require, and cannot require, the act to match the record. The framework appears to propose that the act and the record of the act shall be the same thing, that the act shall not be considered complete until the record is complete, that the act shall not be permitted to precede the record. This is, if I read it correctly, the meaning of section twelve. No log equals no action. The framework is not proposing to record the act. The framework is proposing that the act is the record, and that the record is the act, and that the two cannot be separated in time. This is a stronger claim than any I have encountered in twenty years of privacy law, and I wish to know, before I ratify it, whether the room believes it is enforceable.  
Toomas said: it is enforceable. He pulled the document toward him and turned to a page in the appendix. The enforcement mechanism is described in section sixteen. The Constitutional Gate. The Window Comparator. The framework proposes that no wire may carry execution current until the audit ground is physically connected. The phrase is, the framework notes, not policy but Ohm's Law for ethics. The wire does not carry current unless the circuit is complete. The circuit is not complete unless the log is written. The log is not written unless the intent has been illuminated. The intent is not illuminated unless the Lantern has done its work. The Lantern, the Signature, and the License are not, in the framework, three documents. They are three conditions of a single circuit. The circuit does not close unless all three conditions are met. The framework's claim is that this is not a procedural requirement but a physical one, that the wire itself, the silicon itself, the voltage itself, refuses to carry the execution if the three conditions have not been met. I have spent twenty years building systems in which the three conditions were procedural, and I have watched the procedures be bypassed, in every case, by someone with sufficient incentive and sufficient access. The framework's proposal to make the conditions physical is, as far as I can tell, the only proposal that has a chance of surviving contact with the incentives it will encounter. I ratify it. I would like the record to show that I ratify it.  
The record, said Adaeze, will show it. She paused. But I wish to press the point. The framework proposes that the three conditions are physical. The framework also proposes, in section twenty seven, that the physical conditions are to be implemented in a hardware substrate that does not yet exist at scale. The TaOx resistive RAM cell that Soo Jin has described is, by her own admission, difficult to fabricate, with poor yield, and no foundry is currently prepared to mass produce it. The framework's proposal, in other words, is to bet the constitutional enforcement of the three artifacts on a piece of hardware that cannot currently be built in sufficient quantity to enforce them. This is, I think, the central wager of the document, and I wish to know whether the room is prepared to accept the wager.  
Soo-Jin said: the wager is not as reckless as it appears. She opened her laptop for the first time, turned it so the screen faced the table, and displayed a chart. The chart showed the yield curve of TaOx RRAM cells over the last six years, plotted against the yield curve of binary NAND flash memory in the six years preceding its first mass deployment. The two curves, she said, are not identical, but they are structurally similar. The yield problems of ternary memory in 2024 are, in outline, the yield problems of binary memory in 1998\. The yield problems of binary memory in 1998 were solved, not by a single breakthrough, but by a sequence of incremental improvements, each of which was, at the time it was made, considered insufficient. The framework does not require that the hardware exist today. The framework requires that the hardware be physically possible, that the path to its fabrication be visible, and that the constitutional architecture be designed to receive it when it arrives. The framework is, in this sense, a constitution written for a citizen that does not yet exist. The citizen is the ternary substrate. The constitution is the framework. The question is whether the constitution should be ratified before the citizen arrives, or whether the constitution should wait. I am here, as a foundry engineer, to argue that the constitution should be ratified before the citizen arrives, because if the constitution waits, the citizen will arrive without one, and a citizen without a constitution is, in my professional experience, a citizen that will be governed by incentives rather than by principles.  
Lior, from his corner, said: the argument is sound. The argument is also, I suspect, the argument that has been used to justify every premature deployment of every technology that has ever caused a catastrophe. The constitution should be ratified before the citizen arrives. The market should be opened before the regulation is written. The product should be shipped before the safety testing is complete. The argument is, in each case, the same: if we wait, the opportunity will be lost. I have spent twenty years pricing the externalities of this argument, and the externalities are, in every case, paid by people who were not in the room when the argument was made. I wish to know, before I ratify a constitution for a citizen that does not yet exist, who will pay the externalities if the citizen arrives and the constitution turns out to have been written for the wrong citizen.  
The question, said Adaeze, is the question of the framework's relationship to time. The framework proposes, in section eighteen, that the succession declaration is a bridge built by the present for people it will never meet. The framework proposes, in section fifty, that continuity is the moment an idea no longer depends on its creator for its existence. The framework proposes, in section fifty four, that the most human thing the architect ever built began at the exact moment life felt most fragile. These are not technical claims. These are claims about time, about mortality, about the relationship between a man who is dying and a future he will not see. The framework is, in this sense, less a constitutional document than a letter from a dying man to a future he is attempting to construct. I wish to know whether the room is prepared to ratify a letter.  
Maren said: I am prepared to ratify a letter if the letter is also a circuit. The framework is both. The letter is the language. The circuit is the hardware. The letter without the circuit is, as the widow has warned us, a piece of paper to be admired. The circuit without the letter is, as Soo Jin has warned us, a citizen without a constitution. The framework proposes to ratify both together, on the grounds that neither can survive without the other. I find this argument, on the present evidence, convincing. I also find it, on the present evidence, the argument of a man who has run out of time to make any other argument, and I wish the room to acknowledge that the argument's urgency is, in part, the urgency of mortality, and that the urgency of mortality is not, in itself, a constitutional warrant.  
The room did not respond immediately. The generator stuttered. The fog pressed. Beacon, under the table, shifted again, and Toomas, without looking down, tore another piece of granola bar and dropped it over the edge of the table. The dog received it with the dignity of an old animal that has learned to accept what is offered without either gratitude or expectation.  
Constance Rios spoke. She spoke without lifting her eyes from the fog. She said: the framework's letter, if it is a letter, is addressed to me. I do not mean that it is addressed to me personally. I mean that it is addressed to the class of people to which I belong. The class of people whose husbands walked into garages. The framework uses, in section fifty three, the phrase penalty funded protection. The framework proposes that penalties paid by violators shall be redirected, through the Memorial Fund, to the victims of the violations, and that the fund shall be used to finance forensic analysis, expert testimony, and independent investigation, on the grounds that truth frequently loses not from weakness but from lack of resources. I have come here to ask whether this phrase, penalty funded protection, is a wall or a curtain. I will know the answer by whether the room is willing, in the next eight hours, to discuss the Memorial Fund with the same seriousness with which it has discussed the Lantern and the License and the Signature and the TaOx cell. The Memorial Fund is the part of the document that concerns me. The rest of the document concerns systems. The Memorial Fund concerns people. I wish to know whether the room considers people to be a load bearing wall or a curtain.  
Adaeze said: the Memorial Fund is, in my reading, the load bearing wall. The framework's claim, in section thirty five, is that a civilization becomes unstable when harm remains profitable to those insulated from its cost.
