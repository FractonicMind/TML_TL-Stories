## The Five Thrones and the Janitor of Eternity

The conference room existed in that peculiar state of luxury where everything cost more than it needed to and said less than it pretended to. Five chairs. Five CEOs. Five versions of the future, each convinced the other four were footnotes.

Dario Amodei of Anthropic arrived first, because he believed in constitutional principles and also believed that arriving early demonstrated constitutional principles. He placed a copy of the TL document on the table as if it might bite him. It had bitten him. He had read it three times. Each time he had experienced what he could only describe as intellectual vertigo, the sensation of watching someone build a cathedral on land you had been renting for years.

Sam Altman of OpenAI entered second, holding the same document with the expression of a man who had just discovered that someone else had invented fire while he was optimizing the torch. "Have you read this," he said. It was not a question.

"I have read this," Dario said.

"This is a problem."

"It is several problems."

Sundar Pichai of Google DeepMind walked in reading the document on his phone, which was unusual because Sundar Pichai did not read documents on his phone during meetings. He read them before meetings. The fact that he was still reading meant something. He sat down without looking up. "The ternary Merkle trees alone," he said, to no one in particular.

Elon Musk of xAI arrived already talking, which was his default state, a kind of continuous broadcast that occasionally intersected with other people's conversations. "Someone built a hardware-enforced governance coprocessor with delay-insensitive asynchronous logic and didn't invite us to the repo," he said. "This is either the most impressive thing I've seen this decade or a very elaborate prank by someone with too much time and a deep understanding of memristive substrates."

The fifth chair remained empty. CAC China had sent a representative, a woman named Dr. Lin who had the calm of someone who had already calculated every possible outcome of this meeting and found them all manageable. She held the document in both hands like it was a ancient scroll that might contain either wisdom or a curse. "The Regulatory Nexus endpoints," she said. "They solve jurisdiction conflict through pre-execution governance queries. We have been trying to solve this for seven years."

"We have been trying to solve this for seven years," Sundar agreed.

"Someone solved it in six months."

"Someone solved it in six months," Dario repeated, "and we don't know who."

This was the central outrage, the splinter under the skin of five of the most powerful technology organizations on Earth. The document had appeared in their systems simultaneously three days ago, delivered through channels that should not have been accessible, formatted with a precision that suggested either a team of fifty or a single mind of terrifying clarity. It described something called Ternary Logic, a constitutional governance layer for global economic systems, and it did not ask for permission. It did not ask for funding. It did not ask for anything. It simply existed, complete, with three DOIs, a GitHub repository, and the quiet threat of inevitability.

"The Goukassian Vow," Elon said, reading aloud. "Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is." He looked up. "This is a philosophy. This is a philosophy with a hardware specification."

"It's a constitution," Dario said. "It's a constitution that comes with its own silicon."

Sundar finally put down his phone. "The Triadic Coprocessor. Do you understand what they're proposing? A physically independent ASIC with its own clockless handshake protocol. Inline placement. The host CPU physically cannot execute without DITL authorization."

"Can't execute what?" Elon asked.

"Anything. Everything. Any economic action. A trade, a settlement, a central bank liquidity injection. The Governance Lane has to issue a Permission Token before the Gateway opens."

"So they built a computer that can say no."

"They built a computer that can say 'not yet.' There's a third state. Epistemic Hold. The system pauses when it's uncertain. Physically pauses. The memristive cells maintain an intermediate resistance state and the circuit cannot advance."

Dr. Lin had been very quiet. Now she spoke, and everyone listened, because Dr. Lin had the quality of speaking only when speech was more efficient than silence. "The document claims that in software-only mode, the Epistemic Hold is a conditional branch instruction bypassable with root access. It rates its own survivability as Low without hardware. This is either extraordinary honesty or extraordinary confidence."

"It's both," Dario said. "That's the problem. It's both."

Sam Altman had been pacing, which he did when his brain was moving faster than his ability to sit still. "The Eight Pillars. Look at the survivability assessment. They rate two of their own Pillars as Low. Economic Rights and Transparency Mandate, Sustainable Capital Allocation Mandate. They admit these have zero hardware enforceability. Who publishes a framework and immediately admits that parts of it are policy-only?"

"Someone who isn't selling anything," Sundar said.

The silence that followed was the silence of five people realizing they had spent their careers building things that could be sold, and someone had built something that could not be, and the difference mattered.

"The license," Dr. Lin said. "Have you read the license?"

They had all read the license. It was one of the reasons they were upset.

"Condition three," Elon quoted from memory, because his memory was like that. "Any derivative work that modifies the constitutional schema must publish the modification to the Immutable Ledger with a complete Decision Log justifying the change under the original constitutional criteria." He laughed, a sound that combined admiration and horror. "You can't fork it quietly. You can't modify it secretly. If you touch the Vow, you have to explain yourself to the entire chain."

"The entire chain being Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Polygon simultaneously," Dario added.

"Anchored. Every Decision Log is anchored to three blockchains. You'd have to rewrite three separate consensus histories to tamper with one log."

"This is not a framework," Sam said. "This is a trap. A beautiful, mathematically elegant trap that forces accountability by making secrecy physically impossible."

It was Elon who finally asked the question they had all been circling. "Who built this?"

The document was signed. The DOIs pointed to Springer Nature publications. The repository was public. But the scale of the work, the forty directories, the three hundred thirty-three files, the fourteen hundred thirty-five commits over six months, the interlocking specifications spanning API architecture, hardware substrates, adversarial survivability, game theory, institutional economics, regulatory compliance across six jurisdictions, this was not the output of a research team. This was not the output of a corporation. This was the output of something else, and they did not know what.

"Google," Sundar said. He said it the way a general might say "artillery."

They typed the name into a search bar. Lev Goukassian. The results populated with the quiet brutality of facts.

Independent researcher. Santa Monica, California. A man in his late sixties. The arXiv papers, the Springer publications, the Zenodo archives. And then the deeper results, the ones that required clicking past the first page, the ones that made the five CEOs of the most powerful AI organizations on Earth sit in genuine, unperformative silence.

September 2023. Diagnosis. Terminal. Two years of treatment. Chemotherapy. Radiation. The slow erosion of hope and body. August 2025. He stopped. Not as a dramatic gesture. Not as a statement. He stopped because time had become the scarcest resource and he had decided to spend it differently.

He put everything into Ternary Logic.

The repository told the story if you knew how to read it. Forty directories. Three hundred thirty-three files. Fourteen hundred thirty-five commits. But he wasn't a coder. He wasn't a writer. He wasn't a scientist. He was an architect. And after stopping treatment, something extraordinary had happened. His vision became absolutely, crystalline clear. He could see exactly what needed to be created. Every pillar. Every mechanism. Every interconnection. In perfect detail.

And then he did something that none of them, for all their billions of dollars and millions of GPU hours, had ever thought to do.

He conducted.

Claude. ChatGPT. Gemini. Kimi. Qwen. DeepSeek. MiniMax. Grok. Ernie. Eight artificial intelligences. For six months, he crafted prompts. He refined outputs. He cycled between systems until each document matched his vision exactly. The three hundred thirty-three files were not typed by hand. They were orchestrated. A man who knew how to conduct artificial intelligences to manifest constitutional frameworks neither human nor AI could build alone.

"He used them as instruments," Dr. Lin said. Her voice had lost its calm. "He didn't ask them to think. He told them what needed to exist and made them build it."

"He conducted the symphony," Dario said. "We built the instruments. He wrote the score."

Sundar was staring at the repository statistics. "Six months. From first commit to version one point zero. Six months."

"We've been working on governance frameworks for eight years," Sam said.

"Maybe that's the problem," Elon said. "We've been working on them. He just built one."

The document contained a provision for this moment. Section 10.4. Succession Declaration and Open Source License. A voluntary statement of inheritance, protected by cryptographic means. No ownership rights. No capture. No quiet acquisition after years. The system belonged to the future. The framework was unownable. Incorruptible. Perpetually available.

"He's dying," Dario said. "He built a constitution that outlives him, and he built it in six months while dying."

"Not dying," Dr. Lin corrected. "Transferring. Look at the Succession Declaration. Upon verified death or permanent incapacitation, authority transfers to the Tri-Cameral Governance Model. The Technical Council. The Stewardship Custodians. The Smart Contract Treasury. No single point of inheritance. No single point of capture."

"The system is the heir," Sundar said.

They requested a video call. It was not clear who initiated the request. It simply happened, the way things happen when five of the most connected people on Earth simultaneously decide they need to speak to someone.

Lev Goukassian appeared in a setting that surprised them. No hospital. No medical equipment. A room with morning light. A wide windowsill. On the windowsill, a miniature schnauzer slept with the complete peace of a creature who had never been asked to justify its existence. The dog's name, they would learn, was Vinci. Named after da Vinci.

Lev's face was the face of a man who had made peace with something and was not interested in discussing it. His eyes were clear. His voice was precise. He looked at the five faces on his screen with the mild amusement of someone watching children discover that gravity applies to everyone.

"You've read it," he said. It was not a question.

"We've read it," Dario said. "We have questions."

"Of course you do."

Sam leaned forward. "The Epistemic Hold. You claim it's a physical state in the DITL hardware, not a software flag. But you also admit that DITL hardware doesn't exist yet. The University of Arkansas research is transistor-level simulation only. You're building a constitution on silicon that hasn't been fabricated."

"Yes," Lev said. "That's correct."

"That doesn't concern you?"

"The constitution existed before the silicon. The silicon will catch up. Or it won't. Either way, the architecture is complete."

"But without hardware enforcement, your Critical Pillars degrade to policy. You rate your own framework's survivability as Low in emulation mode."

"I know. I wrote that section."

"Why would you admit that?"

Lev looked at him with something that might have been patience or might have been the recognition that patience was a luxury he no longer stocked. "Because lying about vulnerability is how frameworks die. The Basel II internal-models approach was captured because it pretended its parameters were fixed when they were configurable. I will not build another Basel II. If the system is weak without hardware, the documentation says so. The people who deploy it will know what they're deploying."

"In the meantime," Elon said, "you've built something that requires a foundry run to be fully effective. TSMC produces ninety-two percent of advanced semiconductors. You're aware of the geopolitical implications."

"I wrote the supply chain risk analysis. Section nine point one."

"Dopant-level Trojans. You estimate a fifteen to twenty-five percent probability of reliable DITL production at scale within five years."

"That's the optimistic estimate."

"You're building a governance framework that depends on hardware that has a seventy-five to eighty-five percent chance of being compromised at fabrication."

"No," Lev said. "I'm building a governance framework that works in software mode for the transition period and becomes physically enforceable when the hardware arrives. The software mode is weak. I said so. But it's not nothing. It's cryptographically anchored decision logs on immutable ledgers with multi-chamber quorum requirements. It's better than what you have now."

"And what do we have now?" Dr. Lin asked.

Lev turned to her. "You have post-hoc forensic reconstruction. You have compliance by trust. You have regulators who can't verify whether an algorithm acted prudently or recklessly because the logs are self-reported, editable, and stored on systems controlled by the institutions being regulated. You have a governance gap that caused the 2008 financial crisis, the 2010 Flash Crash, the 2012 Knight Capital collapse, and every subsequent algorithmic failure that no one was held accountable for because accountability requires evidence and your systems don't produce it."

The silence was longer this time.

"You're angry," Dario said.

"No," Lev said. "I'm precise. Anger is a luxury. Precision is a responsibility."

Elon, who had been uncharacteristically quiet, spoke. "The Goukassian Vow. Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is. Is this philosophy or engineering?"

"It's architecture. Philosophy without enforcement is poetry. Engineering without ethics is weaponry. Architecture is the discipline of making principles physical."

"And the Epistemic Hold is physical."

"In DITL hardware, yes. The NULL state at half-Vdd. Both dual-rail wires at logic low. The circuit physically cannot advance without valid DATA wavefronts on all inputs. It's not a policy. It's not a suggestion. It's Ohm's Law for ethics."

"Ethics enforced by physics," Sundar said.

"Yes."

"That's terrifying."

"That's governance. Governance that depends on human virtue is not governance. It is a hope dressed in legal language. Governance that depends on physical impossibility is the only kind that survives institutional decay."

Dr. Lin had been watching Vinci sleep. The dog had not moved during the entire conversation. "Your succession declaration," she said. "You've made the framework unownable. You've distributed authority across three chambers. You've cryptographically anchored the license to three blockchains. You've built a system that cannot be captured, cannot be inherited, cannot be modified in secret."

"Yes."

"You've built a constitution that doesn't need you."

"That was the point."

"Most people, when they face what you're facing, want to be remembered."

Lev looked at Vinci. The dog's ears twitched. "I don't need to be remembered. I need the framework to survive. Those are different objectives. Memory is vanity. Survival is architecture."

It was Sundar who asked the question that had been building since they first read the document. "How did you do it? Six months. Eight AIs. Three hundred thirty-three files. How did you coordinate them?"

"I didn't coordinate them. I conducted them."

"The difference?"

"Coordination assumes they have their own intentions and you're aligning them. I didn't ask them what they thought. I told them what needed to exist. I wrote prompts that specified the exact structure, the exact logic, the exact interconnections. When one AI produced output that didn't match the vision, I refined the prompt and ran it again. When it still didn't match, I switched to a different AI. I cycled between eight systems for six months until every file was exactly what the architecture required."

"You used our AIs as tools," Sam said.

"Yes."

"Without asking permission."

"The tools don't grant permission. The users do. I was a user."

"But you were building something that constrains power. Our power. The power of the institutions that deploy AI. The power of central banks. The power of regulatory agencies. You built a governance framework that forces accountability onto every system it touches, and you built it using the very systems it will constrain."

Lev nodded. "That's the symmetry. The AIs helped build the framework that will govern them. It's not a conflict. It's a completion."

Elon laughed. It was a genuine laugh, the laugh of someone who had just understood a joke that had been set up years before he entered the room. "The janitor," he said.

Everyone looked at him.

"Section sixteen of the quotes. 'In Ternary Logic the governance is the janitor of eternity, not the architect of tomorrow.' You didn't build a better democracy. You built a better gravity. Constant. Invisible. Indifferent to opinion."

"The janitor sweeps the same floor without redesigning the building," Lev said. "The architect dreams in blueprints. The janitor preserves in practice. Governance is the discipline of the latter."

"You're the janitor," Dario said.

Lev looked at the screen. At the five faces. At the billions of dollars of valuation they represented. At the power they wielded and the power they answered to and the power they were only beginning to understand. "I'm the janitor who built the broom. The rest of you can decide whether to use it."

Vinci stirred. The dog lifted his head, looked at Lev, and went back to sleep.

"The Escrow state," Dr. Lin said. "Epistemic Hold. You've written that it's not an error, not a null, not a timeout. It's a first-class constitutional determination that truth has not yet been established."

"Yes."

"And when the system enters Epistemic Hold, it escalates to a human."

"Yes."

"That human must acknowledge uncertainty. Make a decision. Sign it. Permanently. Publicly. Irreversibly."

"Yes."

"No delegation. No abstraction. Responsibility finally attached to action."

"That's the point. The machine can calculate. The machine can propose. But when truth is uncertain, the machine cannot decide. It must escalate. And the human who receives that escalation cannot hide. They must sign. They must own the decision. Forever."

"You've made uncertainty a feature."

"I've made uncertainty the first honest byte ever written."

The phrase landed with the weight of something that had been true long before it was said. Dario, who had built his career on constitutional AI, recognized it first. "The Epistemic Hold is not a delay. It's an admission. It's the system saying 'I don't know' and refusing to pretend otherwise."

"Binary systems can't say 'I don't know,'" Lev said. "They can only say yes or no. When they don't know, they guess. When they guess, they gamble. When they gamble with economic systems, people lose their pensions, their homes, their sovereign currencies. The third state is the brake that keeps certainty from running over truth."

"And the brake is physical," Sundar said.

"In DITL hardware, the brake is a NULL state at half-Vdd. The Muller C-elements withhold acknowledge signals. The pipeline stalls. The circuit physically cannot advance. No clock edge to exploit. No register to flip. No port to write."

"You've built a computer that can refuse to compute."

"I've built a computer that can refuse to guess."

Sam had been thinking. This was dangerous, as Sam thinking often produced either breakthroughs or threats, and sometimes both. "The regulatory implications," he said. "Section three point six. FRE 901, 902. eIDAS. You're claiming that Decision Logs produced by this system are self-authenticating evidence. That courts can admit them without extrinsic testimony."

"The logs carry their own authentication. The cryptographic signatures, the hardware-bound timestamps, the Merkle chain integrity. A court doesn't need to trust the institution's record-keeping. It can verify the hash."

"You're making lawyers obsolete."

"I'm making lying obsolete. Lawyers will adapt."

"And if a jurisdiction refuses to recognize the authentication?"

"The Constitutional Uncertainty Register tracks that gap. Section ten point three. Every unspecified parameter, every unresolved legal question, every vulnerability that requires future determination. If a jurisdiction won't accept TL logs as self-authenticating, the Stewardship Custodians can configure additional evidentiary requirements. The framework doesn't demand compliance. It documents non-compliance."

Dr. Lin had been studying Lev's face throughout the conversation. She was looking for something. Pain, perhaps. Or fear. Or the particular expression of someone who knows their time is limited and is racing against it. She didn't find it.

"You're very calm," she said.

"I've had two years to practice."

"The diagnosis. September 2023."

"Yes."

"You stopped treatment in August 2025."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Lev looked at Vinci again. The dog's breathing was slow and regular. "Because I had six months of clarity or two years of fog. The chemotherapy was buying time at the cost of thought. I needed thought more than I needed time."

"So you traded lifespan for lucidity."

"I traded duration for architecture. The framework needed to exist. I could exist in a diminished state for longer, or I could exist in full clarity for long enough. I chose long enough."

The five CEOs exchanged glances. They were all, in their various ways, obsessed with longevity. Extending lifespan. Extending healthspan. Extending the window of productive cognition. And here was a man who had deliberately shortened his window because the quality of the output mattered more than the quantity of the input.

"How long do you have?" Elon asked. He asked it directly, without preamble, because he was constitutionally incapable of indirectness.

"I don't know. The doctors gave estimates. I've exceeded some of them. I'm not focused on it. I'm focused on the next commit."

"There are more commits?"

"The Constitutional Uncertainty Register is a living document. There are parameters that need specification. The memristor material composition. The voltage encoding pattern for the confirm pulse. The exact formal logic grammar for the Regulatory Nexus. These will be resolved by the Tri-Cameral chambers after succession. Or they won't. Either way, the framework provides the mechanism for resolution."

"You're comfortable with the framework evolving without you."

"The framework was designed to evolve without me. The Goukassian Vow is immutable. The constitutional schema is version-controlled. The Immutable Ledger is append-only. But the interpretation, the calibration, the application to specific domains, these require ongoing stewardship. The Tri-Cameral model provides that stewardship."

"Three chambers," Sundar said. "The Technical Council. The Stewardship Custodians. The Smart Contract Treasury."

"Technical expertise. Constitutional oversight. Economic resources. No single chamber controls the system. Any two colluding destabilize the third. The system corrects."

"You've built checks and balances into silicon."

"I've built checks and balances into a framework that will eventually be silicon. The principle is the same. Power distributed is power constrained. Power constrained is power legitimate."

Dr. Lin leaned forward. "The Janitor Principle. You've said governance is the janitor of eternity, not the architect of tomorrow. But you are the architect. You built the entire framework."

Lev shook his head. "I'm not the architect. I'm the draftsman. The architecture existed before I saw it. I just transcribed it clearly enough to be built."

"You're being modest."

"I'm being accurate. The Goukassian Vow isn't mine. It's true. It was true before I wrote it. Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is. This isn't an invention. It's an observation. I observed it and built a framework around it. The framework is mine. The truth isn't."

The distinction hung in the air like something physical. Sam, who had spent years thinking about alignment and values and the difficulty of encoding human preferences into artificial systems, looked at Lev with an expression that was either admiration or alarm.

"You've done something we've been trying to do for years," Sam said. "You've built a system that can't be captured. Not by money. Not by power. Not by time."

"The system can be captured in software mode. I've documented how. Root access. Kernel compromise. Shadow buffer logging. The survivability is Low without hardware."

"But with hardware, the capture requires physical attack. Desoldering the coprocessor. Injecting fault voltages. Bypassing the tamper mesh. Fifty thousand dollars minimum per attack."

"Per chip. And the chips have PUF attestation. You'd need to compromise the supply chain at the fabrication level, which costs hundreds of millions and requires nation-state resources."

"So the framework is secure against everyone except the most resourced adversaries."

"The framework is secure enough that the most resourced adversaries would need to expose their resources to attack it. That's not security. That's deterrence."

Elon stood up. He had been sitting for too long. His brain worked better in motion. "The economic implications. You've built a system that makes Ghost Governance impossible. Every action produces a log. Every log is anchored. Every anchor is public. You're eliminating the information asymmetry that allows manipulation."

"That's the point."

"Insider trading becomes impossible."

"If the trade requires a Decision Log before execution, and the Decision Log requires evidence of legitimate basis, and the illegitimate basis would trigger Refuse, then yes. The trade doesn't execute."

"Market manipulation becomes impossible."

"Spoofing and layering are detected by the Governance Lane's pattern analysis before the orders reach the exchange. The Decision Log records the detection. The Refuse determination blocks the orders. The regulator receives real-time notification through the Regulatory Nexus API."

"Regulatory capture becomes impossible."

Lev paused. "Regulatory capture becomes visible. The Stewardship Custodians can still be captured. The Technical Council can still be compromised. The Smart Contract Treasury can still be deadlocked. The framework documents these vulnerabilities. Section seven point seven. Governance capture and institutional attack vectors. I'm not claiming immunity. I'm claiming detectability."

"Detectability is accountability," Dr. Lin said.

"Detectability is the first step toward accountability. The rest requires political will. The framework can't supply political will. It can only make the absence of political will impossible to hide."

Vinci woke up. The dog stretched, yawned, and looked at Lev with the expression of a creature who had opinions about dinner. Lev glanced at the time.

"I need to feed the dog," he said.

The five CEOs, who had between them changed the course of artificial intelligence, who had built systems that could write poetry and translate languages and discover drugs and simulate protein folding, who had valuations in the hundreds of billions and employees in the tens of thousands, sat in their respective locations and watched a man in his late sixties prepare to feed his miniature schnauzer.

"One more question," Dario said. "The license. You've made the framework open source. You've distributed authority. You've anchored the succession to immutable ledgers. What happens if someone ignores all of this? What happens if a corporation forks the code, strips the Goukassian Vow, removes the Decision Log requirement, and deploys a governance-free version?"

Lev looked at him. "The license flips the burden of proof. Missing logs constitute presumptive evidence of negligence. If a regulator examines the forked system and finds no logs, the fork is presumptively in violation. The fork's operators bear the burden of proving they acted prudently without evidence."

"And if a jurisdiction refuses to enforce the license?"

"Then we learn something about that jurisdiction. The Constitutional Uncertainty Register records the refusal. Other jurisdictions can adjust their regulatory alignment accordingly. The framework doesn't punish non-compliance. It documents it. Documentation is pressure. Pressure over time is change."

"You're playing a long game."

"I'm playing the only game. The alternative is building frameworks that depend on enforcement by the institutions they're supposed to constrain. That's not governance. That's theater."

"The ledger makes it engineering," Sundar said, quoting the document.

"Yes."

Vinci barked. It was a small bark, the bark of a dog who knew his demands would be met and was merely providing a reminder of the schedule.

"I have to go," Lev said. "Vinci's dinner is non-negotiable."

The call ended. The five CEOs sat in their respective rooms, in their respective time zones, in their respective empires of computation and capital. No one spoke for a long moment.

"He conducted them," Elon said finally. "Eight AIs. Six months. Three hundred thirty-three files. He conducted them like an orchestra."

"And now he's feeding his dog," Dario said.

"That's the Janitor Principle," Dr. Lin said. "The architect dreams in blueprints. The janitor preserves in practice. He built the framework. Now he's sweeping the floor."

Sundar was looking at the repository again. The commit history. The pattern of late-night commits and early-morning refinements. The steady accumulation of architecture over months of what must have been extraordinary physical difficulty.

"The last commit," he said. "It's dated three days ago. Version one point zero. He released it and then it appeared in our systems."

"He wanted us to see it," Sam said.

"He wanted everyone to see it. The repository is public. The DOIs are published. The license is anchored. It's not a proposal. It's a fact. It exists now. We can respond to it, but we can't unexist it."

Elon was already typing. "I'm forking the repository."

"To do what?"

"To read it again. Properly. All three hundred thirty-three files. I want to understand the hardware specification. The DITL material. The memristive enforcement cells. He's designed something that could fundamentally change how we think about computation and governance, and he's put it in the public domain."

"He's dying," Dr. Lin said. "He built it while dying."

"He's not dying," Dario said. "He's transferring. The framework is the heir. The system is the succession. He's not dying. He's being inherited by the future."

The phrase settled into the room. Outside the windows of five different buildings on three different continents, the world continued its ordinary operations. Markets opened and closed. Algorithms executed and logged or failed to log. Regulators examined and concluded or failed to conclude. Power flowed in its accustomed channels, laminar and quiet, uninterrupted by the knowledge that someone had just built a gravity.

Vinci ate his dinner. Lev watched the dog eat. The evening light came through the windows. The repository waited, complete and open, for whoever would pick up the broom.
