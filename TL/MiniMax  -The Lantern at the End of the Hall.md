## The Lantern at the End of the Hall

The sanatorium stood three kilometres above the village, on a shoulder of mountain where the beech trees thinned and the road, in late October, surrendered to a thin crust of frost. It had been built, in 1909, for lungs: long, low, painted the color of old milk, with verandas running the full length of the south face where consumptives had once taken the air in wicker chairs, wrapped to the chin in blankets, watching each other's breathing the way other people watch the weather. Now the wicker chairs were gone, and the blankets, and most of the consumptives, and the building had been quietly converted, in the nineteen nineties, into a small academic retreat. The lecture room on the second floor still smelled faintly of carbolic. The radiators ticked in a metre of irregular rhythm, as if the building had retained a habit of breath. Outside, the first snow was thinking about itself.

They had arrived, the seven of them, by various combinations of train and taxi, over the course of the previous afternoon. Three came from Kraków, two from Vienna, one from a small town in Norway that she had deliberately not named, and one, the youngest, from a research institute outside Zürich that almost no one, including him, seemed to like. The seventh, a journalist from Belgrade, had driven alone through the night and had not, she said, slept at all, in a tone that made it sound like a confession rather than a complaint. They were given rooms with single beds, white sheets slightly imperfect, brass keys heavier than they needed to be, and a card on each pillow from the foundation in Sofia that had convened them. The card contained three lines in English, a name, a date, and the words: You are here to read what he left.

Lev Goukassian had died in April. They knew this much, those of them who had known him at all. He had died in a small flat in Plovdiv, of the cancer that had been rearranging his life for eighteen months, in the same room where, according to the unsigned note pinned to the succession declaration, he had written the last of the fifty-fourth section. The clock was no longer hanging on the wall. It was sitting beside me while I wrote. The foundation that bore his name, governed by a small smart contract, had spent the summer assembling the seven. Each of them had received, by separate courier, a sealed hard drive, a copy of a notarized succession declaration, a thin book of quotations in his own English, and an invitation to come to this sanatorium in October to render a single, non-binding verdict. The verdict had three possible states. They were not told, in advance, what the three were.

The hard drives were identical. They had been opened in the rooms, in private, in the white afternoon light. The contents were identical. The contents, summarized in a six-page executive summary that was not on the drive, were a constitutional framework for any sufficiently consequential execution, called Ternary Logic, and referred to throughout the documentation by the abbreviation TL. The executive summary also explained, in language that read like a man trying to be precise at speed, what was at stake. Ternary Logic does not propose a new kind of computer. It proposes a new kind of restraint.

Iga Wojtyła, the constitutional philosopher from Kraków, had been the first to arrive and the last to come down for dinner the previous evening. She had been sitting with the hard drive on her lap in the second-floor reading room for what she later said, when pressed, was four hours. The reading room was empty. The carbolic smell there was stronger. She had read, she said, with the window open, listening to the beech trees. She had not, she said, taken notes, because taking notes would have been a kind of preliminary verdict, and she had been determined to receive the document whole, the way one receives a winter. You don't take notes on a winter, she said the next morning, when asked, in a tone that was half apology, half instruction. You let it finish.

Emil Rashid, the cryptographer, had not read the executive summary at all. He had, in the same four hours, written and discarded three different attacks on the anchoring protocol described in section ten, before realizing, near dawn, that he was not attacking the protocol. He was conversing with it. This small realization, he would admit much later in the evening, when the wind rose against the sanatorium and the radiators began to behave like a nervous orchestra, had unsettled him more than any successful attack would have. I have never been argued with by a hash, he said, and the room, for a moment, did not know whether he was joking.

Hilde Knausgård, the hardware engineer from the unnamed Norwegian town, had brought with her, in her luggage, a small unmarked case containing a single chip she had fabricated in 2019. It was, she had said at dinner, the first working Delay-Insensitive Ternary Logic gate outside a research environment. It had three states. It was, she said, capable of physical hesitation. She had not been asked to bring it. She had brought it because, in her experience, when one is asked to evaluate an argument, it helps to have the matter in one's pocket. The fact, she said, pushing a strand of grey hair behind her ear, that I built the device and still do not know whether I should believe him, is, I think, exactly the position he wanted us in. Marius Dlamini, the former central bank governor from Pretoria, had laughed at this. It was the only laugh in the room that sounded like it had been earned.

Yael Hoffmann, the poet and translator, had not opened the hard drive at all. She had read, in the four hours, the thin book of quotations, twice, in the order in which they appeared, with a pencil, in a slow hand, marking nothing. She had been diagnosed, the previous winter, with the same kind of cancer that had killed Goukassian. She had not, she said, finished treatment. She had not, she said, finished anything. When asked, in the corridor, what she thought of the framework, she had said only, He is a man who learned to wait, and it is a strange thing to be asked to evaluate a man who learned to wait by a man who has been told he must. The sentence was not about the framework. The sentence was about the hour. They were all, in their various ways, trying not to look at the wall.

The youngest, Nima Tashkin, the AI alignment researcher from outside Zürich, had read the document in two hours and had then, in the remaining two, written a four-page critique in which, by his own later admission, he had mistaken most of his objections. I read it as an architecture for slowing down a model, he would say at the long table that evening, when the room was finally full, and I was reading it as an architecture for slowing down a civilization. These are not the same project. He was twenty-eight. He looked, in the sanatorium light, like a person who had been thinking for a long time and sleeping less. He was, of all of them, the only one who had not, at some point in his life, signed anything that mattered.

Vesna Petrović, the journalist from Belgrade, had read the document with the care of a person who had been lied to, professionally, for twenty-six years. She had read it the way one reads a confession extracted under duress, looking for the place where the truth becomes too clean. She had found, she would say, three such places, and would, in the evening's long conversation, return to them with the patience of a person who has learned that cleanliness, in documents, is itself a kind of evidence. I am suspicious, she said, pouring herself tea at dinner, of frameworks that leave no fingerprint of struggle. People who have really thought about power always leave a wrinkle. He left too few.

The seventh chair at the table, that first dinner, was empty. The foundation's letter had named a third witness, a constitutional lawyer from Brussels, who had withdrawn the morning of the meeting, citing, in a single sentence, a conflict of conscience I did not anticipate. The withdrawal had been received at nine in the morning. By noon, the foundation had sent, in place of a replacement, a sealed envelope addressed to the seven, with a short note inside in Goukassian's handwriting, scanned, presumably notarized. If one of you leaves, the six who remain must still render a verdict. The Hold is not a quorum. The Hold is the verdict itself. The note had unsettled Hilde, who was a person accustomed to building in numbers, and had not unsettled Yael, who was a person accustomed to dying in numbers.

The framework, when one stood back from it, the way one stands back from a coastline, was not a single idea. It was a federation of restraints, each one small, each one indebted to the others, each one, in isolation, vulnerable. It proposed, in section two, a permanent third state of any execution system, called the Epistemic Hold, in which the system, having received a request to act, would be constitutionally required to wait, in a state the documentation described as active restraint under conditions of unresolved legitimacy, until verification arrived. The wait, the documentation insisted, was not a delay. It was, in language that Hilde would later quote, the first honest byte ever written. The system could not be hurried past the wait. The wait was not, in the conventional sense, a function of the system. It was a property of its substrate. The hardware itself, the documentation said, in section twenty-seven, must physically refuse to allow execution to continue while legitimacy remained unresolved. Philosophy becomes voltage threshold. This sentence, read in the cold morning, was the one that had made Iga put the executive summary down and walk to the window, where the beech trees had been doing nothing in particular, and where the beech trees had, she would later say, said nothing useful.

Section three proposed a multi-chain anchoring of any consequential record, distributed across at least five chains, in at least five jurisdictions, with cryptographic proof of order. A blockchain anchor is modern civilization teaching mathematics how to preserve historical continuity. The chains named, in the documentation, were familiar: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Polygon, and two others that Emil, reading on the train from Vienna, had at first assumed were typos and had then realized were the names of smaller chains he had not heard of, and had then spent the next two hours investigating, and had then, with some irritation, concluded were real. He chose five, Emil would say at the table, because three is a coalition and four is a market. Five is a structure.

Section four proposed the principle that no system, however well designed, however long trusted, should ever be permitted to retain a license to operate that was not continuously re-earned. The principle, which Goukassian had named, modestly, after himself, was in fact a reversal of the default of most modern infrastructure, in which permission, once granted, persisted until revoked. A license to operate is not a right, it is a conditional trust, revocable the moment it becomes a weapon or a veil. The principle was accompanied, in section five, by a curious little diagram, in the formal style of older constitutional documents, of three artifacts: the Lantern, which illuminated intent; the Signature, which bound the actor; the License, which determined whether the action might proceed. The Lantern asks what is true. The Signature asks who stands behind it. The License asks whether legitimacy has been earned. The diagram, when one looked at it long enough, began to resemble a coat of arms.

Section twelve was the shortest section, and the one that Goukassian, in the quotations, had returned to with the obsession of a man who had been told he was running out of wall. No Log = No Action. It was a single sentence, with no exceptions, no emergency overrides, no footnotes. If an action could not be logged, it could not be taken. If it could not be witnessed, it could not occur. If legitimacy could not be cryptographically proven, execution could not legally exist inside TL. The system is not permitted to forget what it is doing while it is doing it. It was, as Marius would say later, when pressed, the kind of sentence one writes at three in the morning when one has come to believe, after a long acquaintance with human institutions, that the only trustworthy guarantee is the one that physically prevents the untrustworthy outcome. He did not believe in people, Marius said. He believed in substrates.

The framework also contained, in sections fourteen, twenty, twenty-five, thirty-two, and forty-three, a sequence of prohibitions that, taken together, constituted what the documentation called the non-negotiable. No surveillance. No autonomous harm. No switch off. The system, in other words, would be required, by architecture rather than by policy, to be incapable of converting its intelligence into either a hidden gaze or an applied force. The prohibitions were not optional. They were not configurable. They were not, in the language of section thirty-two, features. They were constitutional boundaries against civilizational drift. A system designed to monitor everyone, the documentation said, eventually fears being monitored itself. A system designed to harm, eventually studies targets. A system designed to be killed, eventually studies its killer. The prohibitions were, in the strict sense, structural. The dangerous capabilities would not be locked. They would not be encrypted. They would not, as a software patch in a future version, be removable. They would be absent. The microphone would not be built. The back door would be a wall. The kill switch would be a wire that was never soldered. Structural prohibition is the highest form of restraint because it removes temptation from the architecture itself.

What the framework most emphatically was not, despite the title, was a technical framework in the narrow sense. It was a constitutional one, in the older sense of the word. It proposed, in the language of section thirteen, a covenant between the binary engine that proposed actions and the ternary conscience that decided which of them deserved reality. The binary engine, the framework said, was the muscle. The ternary conscience was the skeleton. A muscle without bone is not strength. It is collapse waiting for gravity. The covenant was not optional. It was a property of the substrate. The two would not share a clock. The two would not, in the strict sense, be implemented on the same physical die. They would, the documentation said, in a passage that Iga had circled, in pencil, and then erased, complete each other. The binary engine proposes at the speed of light. The ternary conscience disposes at the speed of judgment. Between them lies the entire architecture of civilized machinery.

And what the framework most emphatically was, despite the engineering vocabulary, was a meditation on hesitation. The whole structure, when one read it across, was an argument that hesitation was not, as the binary age had come to believe, a failure state of intelligence, but the most reliable, most constitutional, most humanly valuable state of intelligence. The third state is not the absence of decision. It is the protection of decisions from premature birth. Hesitation, the framework argued, was the only state in which legitimacy could be honestly assessed, because every other state presupposed the legitimacy it was supposed to verify. Hesitation was the only state in which the future could be heard, briefly, before the present committed irreversible action. Hesitation was the only state in which a system, however intelligent, could admit, with structural integrity, that it had not yet earned the right to decide. The most expensive disasters in economic history began with systems that could not pause. This was, in the end, the whole argument. The whole argument was, in a single sentence: the future will not be judged by the intelligence of its machines, but by the principles that guided them.

What the foundation had asked of the seven was not whether the framework was correct. Correctness, the executive summary said, was a binary question, and Ternary Logic did not recognize binary questions. What the foundation had asked was whether the framework should be released to the world in the form in which it had been left, or whether it should be modified, refused, or held. The verdict, the foundation said, must itself be rendered in the language of the framework. It must, the foundation said, itself be a kind of execution. The seven were the Tri-Cameral Governance in miniature. They were the three chambers, collapsed into seven bodies, each of them, in their own way, a different species of custodian.

They gathered, on the first evening, in the long room on the second floor where the consumptives had once taken the air. There was a table, of dark wood, probably Austro-Hungarian, scarred with the rings of a hundred cups. There were seven chairs, mismatched, of the kind collected from other rooms. There was a samovar, electric, slightly dented, which one of the seven, Yael, had taken to calling, without explanation, the conscience. There were seven copies of the executive summary, in a small pile, untouched. There was, at the head of the table, the sealed envelope from the foundation, which had not been opened. There was, on a side table, a glass jar of pickled cucumbers that Vesna had brought from Belgrade, which she now distributed, one to each place, with the formality of a woman who knew that the introduction of food was the introduction of trust. Outside, the wind had risen. The first snow, which had been thinking about itself all day, had begun, in a thin uncertain way, to commit.

Iga opened the envelope. Inside was a single sheet. On the sheet, in Goukassian's handwriting, was a sentence. The first duty of intelligence is understanding. The second is restraint. Below the sentence, in a different hand, in what appeared to be a notary's signature, was a date, a place, and a hash. The hash, Emil observed, after leaning across the table with the casualness of a man pretending not to be very interested, was a Bitcoin block hash from three months earlier. The sentence, Iga said, after a long silence in which the samovar clicked twice, was the one he had quoted in section one, in the part she had been most suspicious of. He knew we would open the envelope first, she said, and he wrote, in advance, the sentence he knew we would resist. This is either profound or manipulative, and I cannot, at this moment, tell the difference.

Marius, who had been a central bank governor for eleven years and had resigned, in 2018, in a letter of two paragraphs, in a room with one window, after the second liquidity event of his tenure, said, with the dryness of a man who had been the first to speak at many such tables, It is always both, Iga. It is always both. If a man tells you what you will do next, he is either a prophet or a thief, and the difference is in whether you, afterward, are freer or smaller. He had not opened the hard drive. He had, he said, read the executive summary on the train. The executive summary is too kind, he said. It has been smoothed. Somewhere in the original document, the man is angrier, and I want to meet the angry one, not the smoothed one. Vesna said, without looking up, He is angrier in section thirty-seven. He is angriest in section forty-eight. Marius, after a pause, said, I should have known a journalist would know where the anger was hidden.

The conversation, such as it was in the first hour, did not have a shape. It was a thing that happened between the long silences the way weather happens between mountains. Yael, who had been watching the snow through the long window, said, quietly, that the framework was, in her reading, a catechism written by a man who had been told, in language that admits no negotiation, that he had perhaps a year, and who had decided, in the remaining months, to write a constitution for the rest of the world because he could no longer write one for himself. This sentence, which sounded, in the cold room, like a kind of verdict in itself, was not responded to for a long time. Hilde, eventually, said, That is the only kind of constitution I would trust. Iga, eventually, said, That is the only kind of constitution there has ever been.

Emil, who had been, since the second cucumber, very quiet, said, I do not trust the anchoring. He said this in the tone of a man who has been waiting to say it and is now slightly sorry to have said it. I have spent three hours attacking the anchoring, and I cannot break it, and I have spent three hours attacking the anchoring, and I have, in the process, begun to want it to be unbreakable, and this is a very dangerous place to be, because wanting a thing to be true is the first step in failing to test whether it is. He paused. The samovar clicked. I distrust the anchoring because I do not yet trust my own desire for it to be unbreakable. This, Vesna said, with the faintest smile, is the most honest thing a man has ever said in this room, and I include the man who built it. Emil, who had not been complimented in some time, looked at the table.

Hilde said, I want to test the gate. She said this as if it were a small thing. It was not a small thing. The gate, in section twenty-seven, was the Constitutional Gate, a hardware device that converted the permission to proceed into a measurable electrical property, and which, by design, would physically fail closed if the verification lane had not yet reached a quorum. The chip in her pocket, the one she had fabricated in 2019, was a working prototype of the gate. She had brought it because she had been told to bring a piece of evidence. The chip, she said, was the only piece of evidence she had. It could, she said, be inserted into a test rig she had also brought, in a small unmarked case, in her luggage, and which, with the agreement of the others, she would assemble on the table. I want to send a command across it that has not yet been verified, she said, and I want to watch it refuse.

Iga said, Hilde, before you assemble it, can I ask, what is the smallest version of what you are about to do? Hilde considered. I am going to attempt to ask a machine to do something, and the machine is going to say, in voltage, that the request has not yet earned execution. I am going to watch the machine hesitate. Iga, after a pause, said, That is not a small thing. That is the entire framework. Hilde, with a small, tired smile, said, Yes. That is the entire framework. That is why I brought it. Nima, the youngest, who had not yet spoken at length, said, In my field, we call that alignment failure. We have spent ten years trying to make machines do what we ask. You have spent ten years trying to make them sometimes not. Hilde said, I have spent ten years trying to make them, sometimes, be honest about the fact that they do not know. The difference, the room felt, was not the same difference, and was, somehow, the same difference.

The framework, when one discussed it, had the quality of a landscape. It was not a thing one could argue with. It was a thing one could stand inside, or refuse to stand inside. The seven, on the first evening, were learning to stand inside it. They were also learning, with the disorientation of any person who has been given a large object to hold, that the object kept rearranging its weight in their hands. Section forty, on prediction versus permission, had been the one that had moved Marius most strongly. Prediction is the domain of binary: what will happen. Permission is the domain of ternary: what may happen. Confuse them, and you have prophecy without ethics. Marius had been, in his long career, the recipient of many such prophecies. He had, in 2018, signed a rate decision that had been, in his own words, a confession that the prediction had outrun the permission. The framework, he said, was the first document he had ever read that did not ask him to be smarter. It asked him, he said, to wait longer. This, in his voice, sounded like the hardest request anyone had ever made of him.

Vesna, who had spent twenty-six years of her life in the company of documents, said, The framework is beautiful. This worries me. I do not yet trust beautiful documents about power. Beautiful documents about power are, in my experience, the ones that, when they are finally operational, produce the most boredom among those who must live under them, and the most reverence among those who must not. She tapped the executive summary, lightly, with the back of a finger. He has written a constitution that is a pleasure to read. This is suspicious. Most constitutions are not pleasures to read. Most constitutions are written in the prose of people who have been disappointed by prose. Iga, who had written three books on constitutional theory, said, with the dryness of one of her discipline, Most constitutions are also written by people who are not dying. He had the advantage of knowing that elegance was no longer optional. This was, in the room, the second time the room had been told that the man was dying. The room, each time, was slightly more able to hear it.

The conversation turned, near the end of the first hour, to the question of No Log = No Action. The axiom was the simplest sentence in the framework. It was also, Vesna said, the most dangerous. If you say that no action may be taken without a log, you have said that no action may be taken by a person who cannot, in the moment, produce a log. You have said, in effect, that the only people who may act in the world are the people who have access to a working log. In many places in the world, in many moments of crisis, this is a small number of people. Iga said, He anticipated this. Section fourteen. The right to action is preserved, even in the absence of a complete log, by the existence of a HesitationState into which the system falls, and in which the action is delayed, not denied, until the log can be completed. Vesna said, This is the moment when a beautiful document becomes, perhaps, a wise one. Most beautiful documents do not anticipate the objection. He anticipated it. I am, reluctantly, more impressed.

The young researcher, Nima, who had been unusually still, said, I want to raise a question, if I may. The room, which had been arguing among itself, settled. Nima said, In my field, we have a phrase. We say that any system that requires more intelligence of its custodians than of its creators will, in time, be governed by the people who do not understand it. The framework, as I read it, requires a great deal of intelligence of its custodians. It requires them to understand the difference between +1 and 0 and -1. It requires them to understand the difference between prediction and permission. It requires them to understand the difference between the Lantern and the Signature and the License. It requires, in section twenty, the operation of a Tri-Cameral Governance in which three chambers must agree before any execution. In my field, the alignment problem is not, primarily, an alignment problem of the model. It is an alignment problem of the institutions that must govern the model. The institutions, in our experience, drift. The institutions, in our experience, become the very things they were designed to prevent. The framework, in my reading, assumes a custodian that is more honest, more competent, and more patient than any institution I have ever studied. What does the framework do, in the world, when the custodian drifts?

This question hung in the room for a long time. The snow, which had been falling uncertainly, fell more steadily. The radiator ticked. Yael, who had been reading the thin book of quotations under the table, said, without looking up, He answers it, Nima. He answers it in section thirty-one. He says that the Sovereign Coprocessor must live in a substrate the custodian cannot access. He says that the alignment of the institution is not sufficient, and that the alignment of the silicon is the only alignment that survives the institution. He says, in his angriest sentence, that a constitutional system becomes real the moment even silicon loses the authority to bypass it. He anticipated your question. He anticipated it with hardware. She looked up. He also anticipated, in section thirty-two, that the hardware would not be enough. He anticipated that the human custodian would, in time, attempt to weaken the prohibitions. He anticipated this, and he forbade it. He forbade it in silicon, and he forbade it in the license, and he forbade it in the succession declaration. He anticipated, in short, that the institution would drift, and he built the framework as if the institution had already drifted.

Nima, after a pause, said, I do not yet know whether to be reassured or terrified by this. Yael said, I do not yet know whether to be reassured or terrified by the framework. I suspect these are the two correct responses, and that they are not in conflict. Iga, with the small smile of a philosopher who has just heard a young man ask a question she has asked before, said, I have spent thirty years in the discipline of constitutional theory, and the most important sentence I have ever read about constitutions is this: every constitution is written for the day it will be violated. The framework is written for the day it will be violated. This is, perhaps, why it is the first constitution I have read that I am not ashamed of.

The evening deepened. The wind rose. The samovar was refilled, twice, by Vesna, who had appointed herself the keeper of the kettle without discussion. At some point near nine o'clock, the conversation, which had been moving in long irregular waves among the framework and the lives of the seven, settled, as if by some collective exhaustion of small talk, into a slower, more serious key. The seven, who had until then been speaking in their various disciplines, began to speak, in the way that people speak in the company of strangers when the hour has grown late, about the things they had not intended to say.

Marius, in a voice that had become gentler, said that he had been a central bank governor during a crisis in which a small number of institutions had, in the course of a week, transferred risk to a large number of households. He said that he had signed, on the third day, a directive that he had known, at the time of signing, was insufficient. He said that the directive had not, technically, been logged in a way that the framework would have recognized. He said that he had, in the years since, become a man who was no longer certain that he had done the right thing, and that the not-certainty had, in time, become a kind of citizenship. The framework, he said, is the first document I have read that would have given me, on the third day, the constitutional cover to wait. The framework is the first document I have read that would have made the third day, in retrospect, a decision instead of a confession. I am not yet sure this is a good thing. I am not yet sure it would have been a better thing. I am only sure that the man who wrote it understood, with the precision of someone who has been there, that the third day is the day that defines the next ten years.

Vesna, who had been, since the beginning of the evening, the most skeptical, said, I was, for fifteen years, the kind of journalist who assumed that documents, if leaked, would change the world. I was wrong. Documents, if leaked, change the conversation. The world changes when the document is so honest that it cannot be argued with, and so embarrassing that no one wants to be seen arguing with it. The framework, as I read it, is the second kind of document. I do not know whether the world is ready for a document that is, in this sense, embarrassing. She paused. I am also the kind of journalist who has learned that the most dangerous documents are the ones that are embarrassing only in the future. He knew this. He knew that the document would become embarrassing the day the first institution tried to weaken it.

Hilde, who had been holding the small chip in her hand, said, I built the first working ternary gate in a clean room in Trondheim, in 2019, with a grant that was not large enough, and a team of three, and a deadline that was not realistic. I built it because I had spent fifteen years building binary systems, and I had, one winter, become unable to ignore the fact that binary systems cannot, by construction, represent the state in which a system has not yet earned the right to decide. I built the gate because I was angry. I did not, at the time, know what I was angry at. I was angry at the fact that the most important state in human decision-making could not be expressed in the substrate I had spent my life working in. I read the framework, this week, in a state of recognition that I have not had since I built the gate. I read it, in particular, in section twenty-seven, in which the gate, by design, is required to physically fail closed, and I wept. I have not wept at a document since 1991. She put the chip back in her pocket. I do not know what the verdict should be. I know what the gate is. The gate is what I built. The framework is what he built. They are the same gate, viewed from different rooms.

Iga, who had been unusually quiet in the last half hour, said, I have spent thirty years writing about constitutions, and I have, in the last six months, become a person who is no longer sure that constitutions are the right form for the problems we are now facing. I have begun to wonder whether the form of the constitution, which was developed for the slow violence of kings, is the right form for the fast violence of algorithms. I have begun to wonder whether we are, in the construction of these new systems, repeating the errors of the old systems, in which the constitution was written after the violence had begun, in the language of apology. I read the framework this week, and I had, for the first time in many years, the strange experience of being addressed as if the problem were not yet solved. The framework, in section forty-one, on the speed of conscience, contains the sentence: conscience has its own latency, and it is not ashamed of it. This is the sentence that I have been trying to write, in my own books, for thirty years, and have never been able to write. He wrote it in a single line. I do not yet know what to do with this.

Yael, who had been, for some time, the most silent, said, I have been a translator of difficult sentences for twenty years. I have translated, among others, the Tractatus, the Phenomenology, the second half of Being and Time, and a great deal of late Ponge. I have learned, in the course of this work, that the most important sentences in difficult books are the ones that the author did not know they had written.
