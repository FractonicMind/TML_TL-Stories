The Janitor's Hardware

The second document arrived exactly one month after the first. Same symposium. Same conference room. Same five people staring at a binder like it might contain explosives.

This one was heavier.

"Ternary Logic as Constitutional Substrate," Maya read, her infrastructure architect voice already acquiring that particular flatness she reserved for things that would require explaining to management. "A Triadic Computational Governance Architecture for High Stakes Automated Decision Making."

She let it drop. The thud was more substantial this time. The coffee rippled with what I chose to interpret as fear.

Raj, our procurement officer, had the expression of a man who had just discovered that his favorite vendor's entire product line was about to become obsolete. "Who handed you this one?"

"Same woman from the EU Commission," Maya said. "She found me at the coffee station. She said, and I quote, 'The first document was the constitution. This is the hardware reference implementation. Read it before your silicon vendors convince you it's impossible.'"

"That's a very specific threat," Chen said. He ran autonomous systems architecture for a logistics firm and had the calm of someone who spent his days preventing warehouse robots from forming labor unions. "That's a threat that implies she knows what our silicon vendors are going to say."

Dani, our federated learning coordinator and the only one among us who seemed genuinely excited by these interruptions to our regularly scheduled symposium boredom, was already flipping pages. "Oh. Oh, this is different. This isn't software governance. This is hardware. This is actual circuits."

"Read it," I said. I am the CEO. Of nothing you've heard of. A mid sized AI infrastructure company that provides computational plumbing for other people's ambitious disasters. I had come to this symposium expecting panels about responsible innovation and had instead received two binders that collectively proposed rebuilding the foundations of computation. "Read the abstract."

Dani read. "This report analyzes Ternary Logic as a comprehensive computational governance architecture for high stakes automated decision making systems, evaluating its capacity to operate across software, hardware, and institutional layers. The foundational innovation, the Epistemic Hold state, enables structured uncertainty management through formal computational hesitation, physically realized via Delay Insensitive Ternary Logic hardware."

"Stop," Raj said. "Delay insensitive ternary logic. That's a real thing? That's not a phrase he made up?"

"It's a real thing," Maya said, her voice distant. She was already searching on her laptop. "Asynchronous circuit design. It's been around for decades. It's just never been used for governance. It's been used for low power devices and radiation hardened electronics and niche applications where clock distribution is a nightmare. But he's proposing..."

"He's proposing using it to force computers to hesitate," Chen finished. "Physically. Electrically. He wants to build circuits that literally cannot proceed when they're uncertain."

The abstract continued, and Dani kept reading, her voice acquiring an almost reverential quality. "The binary framework forces every decision into a false dichotomy: either execute with available, potentially insufficient evidence, or suspend indefinitely without structured resolution mechanisms. The Epistemic Hold transforms hesitation from systemic liability to measurable, auditable, and economically valuable risk management function."

"Transformative," I said. "He's claiming that hesitation is economically valuable. That waiting is productive. That the time spent not doing things is actually generating value."

"Which is true," Maya said. "In high frequency trading, the flash crash happened because systems didn't wait. They executed on stale signals. They cascaded. A trillion dollars evaporated in minutes because no one had built a circuit that could say 'maybe we should check if this price is real before we sell everything.'"

Chen was reading ahead. "The Hold state is physically enforced by hardware. During zero state assertion, the processor literally cannot generate execution signals, creating irreversible hardware hesitation. The processor physically cannot proceed during NULL maintenance. Execution is electrically impossible."

"That's the most terrifying thing I've ever read," Raj said. "And I've read vendor SLAs."

"Wait," I said. "Go back. He's proposing a third state. Not just one and zero. Not just proceed and halt. A third state that means 'I don't know yet, and I'm not allowed to guess.'"

"Plus one is proceed," Dani read. "Minus one is refuse. Zero is hold. And the hold state isn't a software flag. It's not a line of code that says 'wait please.' It's a physical condition where no valid data tokens propagate through the circuit. The gates literally freeze. Power consumption drops to leakage only. The processor is paralyzed until ambiguity resolves."

Maya had found something. "Delay Insensitive Ternary Logic. DITL. There are papers. Actual peer reviewed papers. People have been building these circuits in research labs for years. Multi threshold transistors. Three stable voltage states. Zero volts for minus one, half VDD for zero, full VDD for plus one."

"Half voltage means hold," I said. "The middle state is literally the hesitation state."

"And because it's asynchronous," Maya continued, "there's no clock. The circuit only advances when handshake protocols complete. When it's in the NULL state, waiting for evidence, it consumes almost no power. The safest state is also the most efficient state. He aligned the incentives."

"He aligned the incentives," Raj repeated slowly. "The thing that kills every governance initiative. The thing that makes every compliance department the enemy of every business unit. The fundamental conflict between safety and speed. He solved it by making safety cheaper than speed."

"Cheaper in power," Chen said. "Cheaper in post facto reconciliation. Cheaper in regulatory penalties. Cheaper in flash crashes that vaporize market capitalization."

Dani had flipped to the historical section. "He traces this back to Łukasiewicz. 1920. Three valued logic. The third value was 'possible' or 'indeterminate.' Philosophical. Then Kleene in 1938 made it about computation. The third value was 'undefined' or 'non terminating.' Then Post made it algebraic. But all of them, all the classical ternary logic, treated the third value as a deficiency. Something missing. Something unknown. A gap in knowledge."

"But his third value," I said, "is a mandate. It's not 'I don't know.' It's 'I am required to find out before I can act.' It's not passive uncertainty. It's active verification duty."

"The Goukassian Principle," Dani read. "Continuity between conscience and accountability across all system layers. Every decision's ethical reasoning must be traceable from initial intent through technical implementation to final evidentiary record, with no discontinuity where accountability could be lost."

"That's the thing," Maya said quietly. "That's the thing we don't have. We have models that output probabilities. We have thresholds that convert probabilities to actions. But between the probability and the action, there's a gap. A discontinuity. The system 'knows' something internally that doesn't make it into the log. The uncertainty gets rounded off. The ambiguity gets resolved silently. And when something goes wrong, we can't reconstruct what the system actually considered."

"Because it didn't consider anything," Chen said. "It calculated. There's a difference. Consideration requires time. Deliberation requires the possibility of not proceeding. Calculation is just pushing numbers through functions."

The document detailed the hardware implementation. Triadic coprocessor architecture. A separate chip, or chiplet, that sat between the main processor and the outside world. The main processor did whatever binary calculations it wanted. Proposed actions. Generated outputs. But those outputs didn't go anywhere until the triadic coprocessor validated them.

"The coprocessor intercepts execution signals," I read aloud. "It evaluates each proposed action against constitutional rules. It queries oracles. It verifies evidence. And it only generates a plus one token, authorizing execution, when all conditions are satisfied. If uncertainty exceeds threshold, it maintains NULL state. No token. No authorization. The main processor can calculate all day. Nothing leaves the system."

"It's a bouncer," Raj said. "He built a bouncer for the CPU. The processor can think whatever thoughts it wants. But it can't do anything in the real world until the bouncer checks its ID."

"And the bouncer keeps records," Dani said. "Immutable ledger. Complete decision packages. Intent, deliberation, resolution, evidence hashes. Every Hold state, every trigger condition, every oracle query, every resolution path. All of it logged, hashed, and anchored to multiple blockchains."

"Multi chain anchoring," Maya read. "Bitcoin for permanence. Ethereum for smart contract integration. Polygon for cost efficiency. Three of five active anchors required for global confirmation. The evidence persists even if two chains fail or are legally banned."

"He thought about jurisdictions," I said. "He thought about governments trying to shut this down. He built redundancy into the legal layer."

The tri cameral governance model was next. Technical Council. Nine members. Seventy five percent supermajority required for protocol changes. Stewardship Custodians. Eleven members. Dual nomination to prevent capture. Smart Contract Treasury. Autonomous financial enforcement. No single body could control the system. No single body could terminate it.

"The No Switch Off rule," Chen read. "No governance body can unilaterally terminate TL system operation. Termination requires satisfaction of constitutional conditions that no single body can meet."

"He built a system that can't be turned off," Raj said. "He built an immortal bureaucracy and embedded it in silicon."

"An immortal accountable bureaucracy," Dani corrected. "One that leaves complete records of every decision. One that can be audited by anyone. One that defaults to hesitation when uncertain rather than guessing."

The use cases section was where my CEO brain started doing uncomfortable calculations again. Financial services. Automated AML screening with Hold state suspicious activity report generation. Real time sanctions checking that physically prevented transactions from clearing until verification completed. Basel III capital adequacy monitoring with automatic regulatory notification when ratios breached.

"Banks spend billions on compliance," I said. "Billions on systems that check transactions after they happen. Billions on reconciliation. Billions on regulatory reporting. And he's proposing to make compliance intrinsic to the transaction itself. To make the verification part of the execution rather than a separate process."

"Supply chain verification," Maya read. "Digital twins with certification Hold states. Payment release conditioned on independent verification of labor standards and conflict free material confirmation. The payment literally cannot execute until the certification is validated."

"That's not verification," Chen said. "That's enforcement. That's making the money itself enforce the standards. The payment is the compliance mechanism."

AI governance. Inference governance through triadic coprocessors. Model outputs held until confidence thresholds satisfied. Autonomous systems with hardware enforced hesitation for high stakes decisions. Multi agent coordination using TL as an AI to AI transaction verification protocol.

"He's proposing a language for AI systems to express uncertainty to each other," Dani said. "A protocol for saying 'I don't know yet, wait for me to verify.' A way for autonomous systems to coordinate without assuming trust."

"Central bank digital currencies," I read. "Programmable compliance. Regulatory requirements embedded directly in currency operation. Transaction validity requires compliance verification through Hold states."

"He's proposing to rebuild money," Raj said quietly. "Not just computation. Money. The entire financial system. Every transaction, every settlement, every payment, all of it running on hardware that physically cannot execute without verification."

The quantum computing section made me put down my coffee.

"Post quantum cryptographic migration," I read. "Hybrid signatures combining classical and quantum resistant schemes. Algorithm agility enabling progressive migration without ledger reconstruction. Hash function migration to extended output lengths maintaining post quantum security margins."

"He thought about quantum computers," Chen said. "He thought about the thing that's going to break all our cryptography in ten to twenty years, and he built a migration pathway into the architecture. The system can upgrade its cryptography without rebuilding its evidence chain."

"He thought about everything," Maya said. "Oracle manipulation. Multi oracle consensus with stake slashing. Hardware bypass. Tamper detection and secure boot chains. Governance capture. Tri cameral separation and constitutional immutability. Recursive self modification by advanced AI. Immutable hardware roots of trust with physical fuse based enforcement."

"Physical fuses," Raj said. "He wants to put fuses in the chip that blow if someone tries to modify the governance logic. The coprocessor literally destroys its own ability to authorize actions if tampering is detected."

"That's not a computer," I said. "That's a vault. That's a vault that also does computation."

The document's author section referenced the earlier work. Ternary Moral Logic. TML. The constitutional framework for AI. But this document, this Ternary Logic document, was the hardware substrate. The physical foundation. The thing that made the constitution enforceable at the level of electrons.

Forty directories. Three hundred thirty three files. One thousand four hundred thirty five commits.

"He built two of them," Dani said. "He built TML. Then he built TL, the hardware version, the physical substrate. While dying. While the chemotherapy fog lifted and he had crystalline clarity and he used that clarity to design circuits that force computers to hesitate."

"Call him," Maya said.

"It's eleven in the morning."

"He's in Santa Monica. He has a miniature Schnauzer named after Leonardo da Vinci. He wants to talk to people who read it."

The video call connected after two rings.

The same living space. The same morning light. The same windowsill with the same sleeping dog, gray and peaceful, paws twitching occasionally as if debugging asynchronous circuits in his dreams.

Lev Goukassian looked exactly as he had a month ago. Sharp eyed. Precise. Not smiling but not unfriendly. A man who had allocated his remaining time with the same care he had allocated ternary states.

"You read the hardware document," he said. It was not a question.

"We read the hardware document," I said. "We have more questions. Different questions. The first time we asked about governance. This time we're asking about physics."

"Good. Physics is where governance becomes real. Everything above the hardware layer is negotiable. Software can be patched. Policies can be revised. Institutions can be captured. But the voltage levels at which a transistor switches, the handshake protocols between asynchronous pipeline stages, the physical impossibility of generating a valid token during NULL state. Those are not negotiable. Those are facts."

"You built a bouncer for the CPU," Raj said. "That's what I'm calling it. The triadic coprocessor. It's a bouncer."

Lev smiled. Small. Precise. Allocated. "That's exactly what it is. The main processor can think whatever thoughts it wants. It can calculate probabilities. It can generate proposed actions. It can optimize objective functions. But it cannot affect the world until the bouncer checks its credentials. And the bouncer has very simple rules. Is the evidence complete? Are the oracles in consensus? Do the constitutional mandates permit this action? If yes, proceed token. If no, reject token. If uncertain, no token. NULL state. The processor waits."

"Electrically waits," Maya said. "Not software wait. Not spin loop. Not sleep function. The gates literally freeze. No valid data propagates."

"The gates settle into stable configurations," Lev said. "No metastability. No oscillation. Just absence of tokens. The downstream circuits receive no instruction. They cannot execute because execution requires valid input tokens. It's not that they choose not to execute. They physically cannot. The signal isn't there."

"And power consumption drops," Chen said. "The safest state is also the cheapest state."

"That was the insight. Asynchronous circuits only consume dynamic power when tokens propagate. During Hold state, no tokens propagate. Power drops to leakage only. The system literally saves energy by being careful. The incentives align. Safety and efficiency are not in tension. They're the same thing."

"You solved the operationalization paradox," I said. "The thing where ethics slows everything down so nobody implements it. You made ethics faster by making hesitation efficient."

"I made hesitation productive. The Hold state isn't idle waiting. It's active verification. The system queries oracles. It requests additional evidence. It executes secondary risk models. It prepares the evidentiary package that will accompany whatever resolution eventually occurs. The time spent in Hold is time spent building the accountability record. When the system finally proceeds, the evidence is already complete. When it refuses, the reasoning is already documented."

"The No Log equals No Action rule," Dani said. "That's the same as TML."

"That's the constitutional invariant. It applies across both frameworks. TML implements it in software with cryptographic enforcement. TL implements it in hardware with electrical enforcement. But the principle is identical. No action executes without verifiable evidence of intent and decision. The evidence must precede the action. The log must exist before the world changes."

"And if someone tries to bypass it?" Raj asked. "If someone attacks the coprocessor? If they try to generate fake proceed tokens?"

"Multiple defenses. The coprocessor is physically separate from the main processor. Independent power. Independent clock domain in synchronous implementations, though DITL is asynchronous. Tamper detection triggers immediate lockdown. And the physical fuses. If someone attempts to modify the governance logic, the fuses blow and the coprocessor permanently loses the ability to generate authorization tokens. The system becomes incapable of affecting the world."

"That's irreversible," Chen said.

"That's the point. Constitutional governance must be irreversible. You cannot have a constitution that can be amended by whoever happens to hold the soldering iron. The physical fuses ensure that any attempt to modify the governance rules destroys the governance capability. The system fails safe. It becomes a calculator. It can think but not act."

Vinci stirred on the windowsill. He opened one eye, assessed the conversation, determined that the humans were still discussing voltage levels, and went back to sleep.

"The dog," Maya said. "The dog understands this better than we do. He knows when to pause. He knows when to proceed. He knows when the uncertainty doesn't matter because the sun is warm and the windowsill is comfortable."

"Vinci is an excellent governance architect," Lev said. "He taught me that most decisions don't require immediate resolution. Most uncertainty resolves itself if you wait. The trick is knowing which uncertainty requires active verification and which uncertainty is just the world being the world."

"The Epistemic Hold," I said. "It's not just delay. It's structured delay. Time bounded. Evidence gathering. Resolution paths defined in advance."

"Time bounds are critical. Indefinite Hold is just another form of halt. The system must commit to resolving within a specified window. For high frequency trading, that window might be fifty milliseconds. For trade settlement, twenty four hours. For supply chain verification, seventy two hours. The bound is domain specific but always present. The system cannot remain in NULL forever."

"And if resolution doesn't occur within the bound?"

"Automatic transition to minus one. Reject. The transaction fails safe. Better to reject a legitimate transaction than to execute an illegitimate one. The cost of false negative is almost always lower than the cost of false positive in high stakes domains. A rejected trade can be resubmitted with additional evidence. An executed fraudulent trade requires costly unwinding, if unwinding is even possible."

"The Goukassian Principle," Dani said. "Continuity between conscience and accountability. That's the thing that feels most different from how we build systems now. We have conscience. Our models have internal states. They have uncertainty estimates. They have alternative hypotheses. But none of that makes it into the log. The log records the final output. The probability. The action. Not the deliberation. Not the alternatives considered. Not the uncertainty that was rounded off."

"Because logging is expensive," Lev said. "Because storage costs money. Because nobody wants to explain why the system considered seven alternatives before choosing the eighth. Because the narrative is cleaner when the decision appears inevitable. But accountability requires the mess. The uncertainty. The alternatives. The paths not taken. The TL log includes all of it. The complete decision package. Intent, deliberation, resolution, evidence hashes. Everything the system knew, everything it considered, everything it rejected. When something goes wrong, the investigator can see not just what the system did, but what it thought about doing."

"That's terrifying," Raj said. "For the humans. We've spent our entire careers building systems that let us avoid responsibility. The algorithm decided. The model recommended. The data suggested. And you just. You built hardware that makes that impossible."

"I built hardware that makes it visible. The responsibility was always there. The humans who designed the system, who selected the training data, who set the thresholds, who deployed it in production. They were always responsible. The opacity of binary systems just let them pretend otherwise. TL removes the pretense."

"The tri cameral governance," Chen said. "Technical Council, Stewardship Custodians, Smart Contract Treasury. That's not just for the AI systems. That's for the humans too."

"Especially for the humans. The greatest vulnerability in any governance system is the humans who operate it. They can be captured. They can be coerced. They can be replaced. The tri cameral structure distributes authority so that no single human, no single institution, can compromise the system. Seventy five percent supermajority requirements. Dual nomination structures. Staggered terms. Mandatory rotation. The system is designed to survive its human operators."

"And the No Switch Off rule," I said. "It can't be terminated."

"It can be terminated. But only through constitutional processes that require consensus across all three governance bodies. No single actor can flip a switch and make the accountability disappear. The evidence persists. The logs remain. The anchors on public blockchains continue to exist. Even if every human involved in the system were replaced tomorrow, the historical record would remain. The accountability would remain."

"That's the janitor of eternity," Dani said quietly. "Not the architect of tomorrow. The janitor. The one who cleans up."

"The one who ensures that the mess is documented. That the spills are recorded. That when something breaks, we know what broke, when it broke, and who was supposed to be watching. The architects will build whatever they build. Brilliant systems. Powerful systems. Systems that transform the world in ways we cannot predict. My job was to ensure that when those systems do something unexpected, we can trace it back to its source. We can understand it. We can assign responsibility. We can learn."

Vinci stretched on the windowsill. A full body stretch, paws extending, back arching, a small sound of satisfaction.

"He agrees," Maya said.

"He usually does," Lev said. "He's been right more often than I have."

The light in the room had shifted again. The morning was becoming afternoon. The symposium continued somewhere beyond our door, panels about market positioning and competitive differentiation, all of it suddenly sounding like people discussing the arrangement of furniture in a house that was about to be rebuilt from the foundation.

"The ternary logic," I said. "Not just the governance. The actual circuits. The multi threshold transistors. The three stable voltage states. You're proposing to rebuild computation from the transistor level."

"I'm proposing to add a new primitive. Binary computation will continue. Most calculations don't require triadic evaluation. Matrix multiplication. Gradient descent. Image processing. All of that runs perfectly well on binary hardware. The triadic coprocessor handles only the governance layer. The decisions about whether calculated outputs should be permitted to affect the world. The main processor proposes. The coprocessor disposes."

"And the coprocessor is small," Maya said. "Simple. Verifiable."

"Exactly. The attack surface is minimal. The coprocessor implements only the constitutional rules. It has no general purpose programmability. It cannot be repurposed. It cannot be updated without physical access and governance approval. It does one thing, and it does it with electrical inevitability."

"The quantum resistance," Chen said. "You thought about quantum computers."

"Anyone building systems designed to persist beyond ten years must think about quantum computers. The cryptographic migration pathways are specified. The algorithm agility is built into the anchor format. When NIST standardizes post quantum signatures, TL systems can adopt them without rebuilding their evidence chains. The anchors include algorithm identifiers. The verifiers can handle multiple signature schemes. The transition can be gradual."

"You thought about everything," Raj said. It wasn't admiration in his voice. It was something closer to professional jealousy. "Oracle manipulation. Hardware bypass. Governance capture. Recursive self modification. Quantum cryptanalysis. You built defenses against threats that don't even exist yet."

"All of those threats exist. Oracle manipulation happens every day in DeFi protocols. Hardware bypass is the entire field of hardware security research. Governance capture is the history of every institution ever created. Recursive self modification is what machine learning systems do when they optimize their own architectures. Quantum cryptanalysis is a matter of engineering timeline, not theoretical possibility. "I chose documentation. The system documents everything. Every decision. Every hesitation. Every resolution. Every attempt to bypass or compromise. When something fails, and something always eventually fails, the documentation will tell us why. Not guesswork. Not post facto rationalization. Not plausible deniability. Just the record. Complete. Immutable. Verifiable."

"The Goukassian Vow," Dani said. "Pause when truth is uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed where truth is. That's not in the hardware document. That's from TML."

"The Vow is the source code. TML implements it in software. TL implements it in hardware. The words are the same because the principle is the same. The architectural expression differs but the constitutional commitment is identical. Pause when uncertain. Refuse when harm is clear. Proceed only when truth is established."

"And when truth cannot be established within the time bound?"

"Then the system refuses. Better to reject a legitimate action than to execute a harmful one. The cost asymmetry is fundamental. False negatives can be remedied through resubmission. False positives create irreversible harm. The system is designed to err toward safety."

"That's not how we build systems," I said. "We build systems to maximize throughput. To minimize latency. To optimize for the happy path. We treat uncertainty as noise to be filtered out, not as signal to be acted upon."

"I know. That's why I built this. The existing trajectory was toward faster systems with less accountability. More powerful systems with less visibility into their operation. Not a separate layer. Not an afterthought. The foundation."

"The janitor's hardware," Chen said.

"The janitor's hardware. Not glamorous. Not the thing that gets keynote presentations. But the thing that ensures the glamorous systems don't burn down the building. The mop and bucket. The cleaning supplies. The documentation of every spill."

Vinci snored softly. A small sound. Rhythmic. Peaceful.

"He really does understand," Maya said. "He's in Hold state. Low power mode. Waiting for evidence that something requires his attention."

"He's in NULL state," Lev said. "No valid tokens. No action required. Perfect governance."

The call ended. The conference room was quiet. The binder sat on the table, substantial and dense, a hardware reference implementation for a constitutional substrate that might never be built, or might become the foundation of every high stakes system in the world.

"We should build this," Chen said. "Not the whole thing. Not the global financial infrastructure. But a prototype. A triadic coprocessor. Something that demonstrates the principle. Something that shows it's possible."

"Carry the Lantern gently," Dani said. "That's what he wrote. In the TML document. Carry the Lantern gently. As if the light is fragile. As if it needs protection not from enemies but from neglect. From the human tendency to set down heavy things and forget where we left them."

The symposium continued. Panels about scaling. Panels about optimization. Coffee breaks and networking and the careful performance of concern about responsible innovation.

But five people in a conference room had seen something else. A man in Santa Monica, with a sleeping dog on his windowsill, who had spent his last months of crystalline clarity designing circuits that force computers to hesitate. Not because hesitation is efficient, though he proved it could be. Not because hesitation is profitable, though he showed it might be. But because hesitation is the foundation of accountability. The pause between impulse and action where conscience lives. The NULL state between intention and execution where responsibility attaches.

He had built that pause into silicon. Made it electrically inevitable. Created a substrate where uncertainty is not a bug but a feature, not a failure mode but a governance mechanism, not something to be optimized away but something to be documented, verified, and resolved with human judgment.

The janitor of eternity. Not the architect of tomorrow. Just the person who made sure that when tomorrow's architects finished building whatever they built, there would be a record of what they'd done, a way to trace consequences back to choices, a mechanism for holding someone, somewhere, accountable.

The coffee had gone completely cold. No one had noticed. No one had cared.

Outside, the California sun was doing whatever the California sun does, indifferent to ternary logic and asynchronous circuits and miniature Schnauzers named after Renaissance masters. But inside, in the flat fluorescent light of a hotel conference room, five people who had come to a symposium expecting the usual had found something else entirely.

A way to build machines that know when they don't know.

A way to make hesitation productive.

A way to ensure that the lantern keeps burning, not because anyone is required to carry it, but because it's built into the foundation of everything we build from now on.

Carry the Lantern gently. Or better yet, build it into the hardware, so the hardware carries it for us, whether we remember to or not.
